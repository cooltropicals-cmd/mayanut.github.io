Tropical superfood

What is MayaNut?

MayaNut is the roasted seed and pulp of the long-lived tree Brosimum alicastrum. Across Mesoamerica it is known as ramón, ojoche, ojite, guaimaro and breadnut. We use the modern, simple word “MayaNut” to capture that story in one name.

Future product line

Why build a MayaNut brand?

The goal is not just one drink or one cookie. It is a recognizable, trustworthy line of MayaNut products that help fund research, plantings and conservation of the tree’s genetics for decades.

Planned MayaNut Products

Powder • Beverage Bases • Flour • Fruit-based products

All of these products are built around the same tree: Brosimum alicastrum. It has carried many names — MayaNut, ramón, ojoche, ox, ojite, guaimaro, breadnut — but one consistent role: a resilient, calorie-dense food from a living tree.

Our intent is to keep the entire process as simple and transparent as possible:

  • Traceable sourcing from long-term plantations and agroforestry plots where tree survival, genetics and hurricane resilience are constantly recorded.
  • Minimal, honest formulations — drinks and flours that highlight MayaNut itself instead of hiding it behind sugar or caffeine.
  • Real recipes that anyone can adapt at home: tortillas, cookies, bread, smoothies and jams.

Until full production begins, this page serves as a public blueprint. If you grow, bake, formulate or research superfoods, you can already see exactly where MayaNut products are heading.

How to Use MayaNut

What you can do with a bag of powder or fruit

Even before full retail products exist, you can imagine exactly how MayaNut behaves in a kitchen:

  • As a drink — whisk 1–2 tablespoons of MayaNut powder into hot water or milk with a touch of sugar or honey. Chill it over ice for a summer drink or keep it steaming on a cool morning. It leans toward chocolate and roasted grains, without the caffeine.
  • In doughs — blend a portion of MayaNut flour into your tortilla or bread dough (for example, 10–20% of your total flour weight). It adds aroma and color without taking over the texture.
  • In cookies & bars — use the powder like cocoa, swapping part of the cocoa or flour for MayaNut in your favorite recipes.
  • As fruit jam — for growers and experimenters: the ripe fruit pulp can be simmered with sugar and water until thick, then jarred like other preserves.

On the main site’s Recipes section we will continue collecting and refining specific, tested recipes — from “MayaNut Mocha” to “MayaNut Tortillas.” This “Buy MayaNut” page focuses on how those recipes connect to eventual product formats.
