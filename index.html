About The MayaNut Company

Welcome to mayanut.com, home of The MayaNut Company. Founded in 2025 by August Kokus, the project grew out of a fascination with banyan trees, the Moraceae family, and the idea that one underutilized tree could become a global superfood crop.

While exploring Moraceae, August encountered Brosimum alicastrum ‚Äì the maya nut tree ‚Äì whose small, fig-like fruits hide a seed that can be roasted and turned into a rich, chocolatey, caffeine-free beverage base. It felt like discovering a missing piece: a tree that is at once shade, food, and a living carbon-storing pillar in the landscape.

August Kokus

August created The MayaNut Company because he wanted to leave behind something tangible and beautiful for future generations ‚Äì not just an idea or a logo, but real forests and real products. His goal is to turn the overlooked maya nut tree into a respected superfood and a symbol of long-lived tropical agriculture.

The plan is bold: build plantations of Brosimum alicastrum in South Florida, document everything, and share what is learned along the way. It means years of patience while trees grow, roots grip limestone, and canopies expand ‚Äì but that slow time scale is exactly what makes the project meaningful.

By around 2030, the vision is to have at least one functional plantation, at least one acre in size, producing enough seed to support real beverage and powder production. From there, more plantings ‚Äì and more experiments ‚Äì will follow.

Where We‚Äôre Growing

South Florida‚Äôs subtropical climate is especially suitable for maya nut. Winters are mild, humidity is high, and soils range from sandy to limestone, giving us a chance to learn how Brosimum alicastrum behaves outside its native range.

Early experience suggests that locations at or below about 27¬∞N in Florida ‚Äì roughly south of Stuart ‚Äì are very favorable. Trees can likely survive a bit farther north, but long-term trials are needed to understand growth, fruiting, and cold tolerance. Our plantations will document how this superfood tree performs in real-world conditions.

The MayaNut Company exists to make sure Brosimum alicastrum is not forgotten. That means treating it as both a crop and a living library of genetic diversity ‚Äì selecting trees that perform well while preserving the wild range of forms and flavors found in nature.

Our Fascination

From their welded, twisting roots that hug limestone to their dense evergreen crowns, maya nut trees embody the idea of long-term agriculture. Common Names, Languages & Indigenous Use

Brosimum alicastrum has many names across its range, reflecting the cultures that have used it for centuries. In English and global food discussions, it is increasingly called MayaNut ‚Äì a simple brandable name that hints at its deep history and future as a superfood. In Spanish, common names include nuez de ram√≥n, ram√≥n, ojoche, and guaimaro / guayumaro, depending on the country and region.

In Mesoamerica, Indigenous communities have long gathered the fruits and seeds as a staple or fallback food, drying and grinding the seeds into flour or drink bases. In some areas, the tree is known as a kind of ‚Äúbread tree‚Äù ‚Äì a provider of energy-dense food in times when annual crops may fail. These traditional uses are part of what inspires The MayaNut Company: honoring old knowledge while developing new products.

Botanically, two subspecies are recognized: Brosimum alicastrum subsp. alicastrum, more widespread from Mexico through much of Central America, and Brosimum alicastrum subsp. bolivarense, found farther south in parts of northern South America. Both belong to the Moraceae family ‚Äì the same family as figs, mulberries, breadfruit, and jackfruit.

Natural Range & Climate

In the wild, Brosimum alicastrum occurs from southern Mexico through Guatemala, Belize, Honduras, El Salvador, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, and into parts of Colombia and Venezuela. It is typically found in lowland to mid-elevation tropical and subtropical forests, sometimes in relatively dry zones, sometimes in very humid evergreen forest.

This wide range suggests strong flexibility: maya nut can tolerate pronounced dry seasons once established yet still thrive in wetter forests. That flexibility is one reason we believe it has real potential in South Florida and other subtropical regions, where climate change is already reshaping rainfall and temperature patterns.

Growth, Roots & Tree Form

Maya nut trees can eventually reach 20‚Äì40 meters tall in ideal conditions, forming a broad, rounded crown. In youth, a great deal of energy is invested in building structural wood and a powerful root system. Over time, the tree becomes a deep-rooted, hurricane-resistant presence in the landscape.

The roots are especially interesting for growers: they tend to grip and explore limestone and rocky soils, anchoring the tree in place. This makes Brosimum alicastrum well suited to windy, storm-prone subtropical coasts where shallow-rooted trees might fail.

Flowering, Pollination & Fruit Formation

Brosimum alicastrum produces small, spherical inflorescences typical of Moraceae. Like many members of the fig-mulberry family, flowers are tiny and arranged in dense clusters. Male and female structures may appear on the same tree or different ones, depending on genetics.

Pollination is primarily insect-driven, with small flies, bees, and other tropical micro-pollinators visiting the inflorescences. After successful pollination, the fruit enlarges into a small, fig-like drupe with a soft edible exterior and a single seed at the center ‚Äî the ‚Äúmaya nut.‚Äù

Fruit, Seed & Superfood Value

The fruit pulp varies by region: some are sweet, some neutral, some tangy. But the seed ‚Äî roasted, ground, or dried ‚Äî is what has earned the tree its place as a potential superfood.

MayaNut seeds are naturally:

  • caffeine-free
  • gluten-free
  • high in complex carbohydrates
  • rich in minerals including calcium, potassium, and magnesium
  • a source of natural antioxidants

When roasted, they take on chocolate/coffee-like aromas, making them ideal for beverages, baking, and snacks. Indigenous cultures historically roasted and stored them as a drought or famine food, while modern analyses show promising nutritional density.

Genetics, Diversity & Subspecies

The species shows considerable variation in fruit size, seed shape, leaf form, and branching architecture. This diversity reflects both natural evolution across Central America and thousands of years of traditional use. Two recognized subspecies exist today:

  • Brosimum alicastrum subsp. alicastrum ‚Äî widespread in Mesoamerica
  • Brosimum alicastrum subsp. bolivarense ‚Äî reported in northern South America

The MayaNut Company‚Äôs long-term mission includes documenting these genetic differences, preserving unique traits, and identifying the best-performing lines for subtropical cultivation.

Cultivation, Spacing & Watering

In typical agroforestry settings, MayaNut trees grow well at spacings of around 5‚Äì6 meters (‚âà16‚Äì20 feet). Closer plantings can work for early-stage trials, especially when the goal is to observe variation and select the best individuals.

Young trees benefit from consistent watering, mulch, and some shade during the hottest months. Once established, they tolerate drought surprisingly well. In Florida limestone soils, deep watering encourages the roots to explore cracks and anchor firmly.

For irrigation, a simple schedule might include:

  • 2‚Äì3 deep waterings per week during the dry season
  • light biweekly watering during rainy months
  • occasional foliar sprays (optional) if using organic nutrients

Harvesting & Preserving the Fruit

Fruits can be eaten fresh, dried, fermented, or cooked into jams. To preserve fresh MayaNut fruit as jam:

  • Harvest fully ripe orange/yellow fruit
  • Remove the seed gently without breaking it
  • Simmer the pulp with sugar and a squeeze of lime
  • Cook until it thickens into a sweet spread
  • Store in sterile jars

This jam preserves the fruity flavor and provides a way to enjoy MayaNut even before processing the seeds into powder.
