RESEARCH

Brosimum alicastrum is a species of tree in the family Moraceae. It is primarily dominant in the tropical forests of southern Mexico, but also occurs in more scattered populations as far north as Tamaulipas and in western parts of Mexico and Central America, extending into northern South America and parts of the Caribbean, including Cuba and Jamaica. In different regions, it is known as maya nut, breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, or guiamaro.

In its native range, Brosimum alicastrum can reach up to about 40 meters in height, though it is more typically 25‚Äì35 meters tall. In South Florida plantings observed so far, trees tend to remain under 20 meters, growing under a rainfall regime of roughly 65 inches per year. Seedlings do not respond well to intense full sun: leaves may turn yellow when ultraviolet exposure is too strong. Young plants prefer moist, partially shaded conditions and generally tolerate more direct light once they are taller than about 2‚Äì3 feet. Established trees can tolerate periods of drought, especially once their roots reach deeper moisture.

Unlike annual crops that must be replanted, fertilized, and replaced, Brosimum alicastrum behaves like a long-lived forest tree. Productivity does not necessarily decline with age; larger trees can bear more seed than young trees. Deep roots allow access to the water table and help anchor the tree against wind. Because this species evolved in regions exposed to tropical storms and hurricanes, it is naturally suited to hurricane-prone places such as Florida.

Morphology

As a member of Moraceae, Brosimum alicastrum produces many tiny flowers packed into a globose, fused inflorescence. Trees are usually dioecious (separate male and female trees), but monoecious individuals with both male and female flowers on the same tree also occur. The flower heads are somewhat reminiscent of miniature Treculia inflorescences. Phylogenetic studies indicate that the Brosimum lineage ultimately traces back to an African ancestor within the tribe Dorstenieae, with long-distance ‚Äúrafting‚Äù events thought to have brought ancestral material to the Americas.

In Brosimum, a single seed is usually present in each fruit. The ancestral condition for the group was probably a multi-seeded, golf-ball-sized syncarp composed of many small flowers subtended by green peltate bracts around 2 mm in diameter. Over time, Brosimum alicastrum appears to have specialized toward a structure with, in monoecious specimens, one centrally placed pistillate (female) flower surrounded by numerous male flowers in each head. In other individuals, only a single pistillate flower develops. Environmental conditions and stress are thought to influence how sex expression is manifested in different trees.

More research is still needed on this species in localized populations across its range. The MayaNut Company is actively working on acquiring new Brosimum alicastrum seeds from different genetic sources to help maintain sufficient genetic diversity for long-term use in agriculture and agroforestry.

Domestication by the Maya and related cultures appears to have been limited compared with some other staple crops. However, the species shows clear potential for selective breeding into more stable strains that could yield larger, more uniform, and more nutritious seeds.

Historically, Brosimum alicastrum is believed to have been important not only for the Maya but also for other cultures overlapping its range. It was likely used by early Mesoamerican hunter-gatherers as early as 10,000 B.C. Direct written or artistic records of specific recipes and culinary uses are scarce, and few surviving texts clearly describe how it was prepared in ancient times. Today, maya nut is still used as a fodder tree for ranchers in Mexico and as a local food resource in some communities.

Culinary Uses

The seeds of Brosimum alicastrum (maya nut, breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, guiamaro) can be dried, roasted, and ground to make cookies, tortillas, bread, and even a coffee-like beverage. These products all originate from the starch-rich seed inside the fruit. The MayaNut Company is working in South Florida to establish a dedicated Brosimum alicastrum farm, often described as a maya nut plantation, because this tree remains widely underutilized in modern agroforestry systems.

The fruit itself is also edible. It begins green, then turns orange, and finally ripens to a deep red. In the green stage, the pulp is hard and woody, with a mildly bitter, latex-like taste. As it shifts to orange, the texture softens and the flavor becomes noticeably sweeter. Fully ripe, dark red fruits detach easily from the seed. At this stage the pulp is tender and chewy, with a sweetness reminiscent of dried figs or raisins and a subtle tang similar to tamarind, but generally milder and sweeter.

Fresh fruit has a very short shelf life and is best eaten directly from the tree. For wider distribution, the pulp would need to be processed into jams, jellies, or other preserved products. The seed, however, can be stored for several months under optimal dry, cool conditions, which makes it a more practical ingredient for long-term use and transport.
