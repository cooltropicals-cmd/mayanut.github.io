ABOUT THE MAYANUT COMPANY

About The MayaNut Company

Welcome to The MayaNut Company at mayanut.com, created to bring a single underused tree ‚Äì Brosimum alicastrum ‚Äì into the spotlight as a true tropical superfood. In Spanish it is often called nuez de ram√≥n or ojoche. In other regions it goes by breadnut, guayumaro, guaimaro, and more. On this site we unify those traditions under one simple name: MayaNut.

People have eaten the fruits and seeds of this tree for centuries, roasting the ‚Äúnuts‚Äù into a coffee-like drink, grinding them into flour, or storing them as emergency food. Our goal is to translate that history into a modern industry built around respectful agriculture, genetics, and world-class flavor.

Founder ‚Äì August Kokus

The MayaNut Company was founded in 2025 by August Kokus, a South Floridian botanist fascinated by banyan trees, figs, and the entire Moraceae family. While exploring this family, he found Brosimum alicastrum ‚Äì a tree whose fruits resemble a small fig, but whose seed has an entirely different potential.

August wanted to build something that could live on after him: a planted, living world of trees that people actually use. MayaNut offered exactly that ‚Äì a species with genuine food value, deep cultural history, and enough mystery that almost no one in the United States had explored it seriously.

By around 2030, he aims to have a functional plantation of MayaNut trees in South Florida, at least an acre in size, bearing the first real harvests that can be turned into beverages, powders, flours, and more.

Where We‚Äôre Growing

South Florida‚Äôs warm, frost-free climate makes it a natural testing ground for Brosimum alicastrum. The MayaNut Company focuses first on locations at or below roughly 27¬∞N in Florida, where winter cold is rare and evergreen trees can maintain year-round growth.

Further north ‚Äì up toward 28¬∞N ‚Äì the species may survive but still needs long-term trials. Our early plantings explore which microclimates, soils, and layouts give the tree the best chance to become a stable, hurricane-resilient part of the landscape.

Mission & Long View

Our mission is simple and ambitious at the same time: make Brosimum alicastrum impossible to ignore. That means developing an entire chain ‚Äì from genetics and seedlings, to plantations and agroforestry, to MayaNut superfood powders and beverages on store shelves.

We want future generations to recognize MayaNut the way people recognize coffee or cacao today: as a flavor, a story, and a living tree that shaped landscapes and communities. This site is the first chapter in that story.

RESEARCH ‚Äì BROSIMUM ALICASTRUM & INDIGENOUS USE

Brosimum alicastrum ‚Äì Research & Superfood Background

Common Names & Indigenous Use

Brosimum alicastrum has many names. In English and Spanish it is often called MayaNut, nuez de ram√≥n, or ojoche. In different regions it may be known as breadnut, ram√≥n, ox or oox, guayumaro, and guaimaro. All of these point back to the same basic idea: a canopy tree whose fruits and seeds can feed people and animals.

For many Indigenous communities, the tree has been more than a curiosity. Fruits were collected and the seeds roasted, ground, or stored as a reserve food. Roasted seeds can make a warm drink similar to a caffeine-free coffee or cocoa replacement, or they can be turned into flour for tortillas, breads, and porridges. In some areas, families relied on ram√≥n or ojoche as a famine-resilient food when other crops failed.

The MayaNut Company uses the simple, modern name ‚ÄúMayaNut‚Äù as a unifying term for this superfood, while honoring the many local names that kept the tree alive in cultural memory.

Natural Range & Climate

In the wild, Brosimum alicastrum grows from southern Mexico through Central America into parts of northern South America. It appears in lowland and foothill forests, tolerating both humid rainforest conditions and seasonal dry forests where long, dusty seasons alternate with heavy rains.

Once established, the tree can handle dry spells surprisingly well, thanks in part to its deep root system. At the same time it thrives in wetter climates, making it an excellent candidate for tropical and subtropical agroforestry. This flexibility is one reason The MayaNut Company is confident about its future in South Florida.

Growth, Roots & Hurricane Resistance

MayaNut trees can reach 20‚Äì40 meters tall in favorable conditions, with a broad, rounded crown and dense evergreen foliage. Young trees often invest heavily in roots and trunk before showing dramatic canopy expansion. Over time they become serious shade trees, with thick, interlocking roots and, in some sites, buttresses around the base.

Those roots matter: they tend to cling tightly to rocky or limestone-influenced soils, forming a kind of living anchor. That is one of the reasons we see Brosimum alicastrum as an excellent candidate for hurricane-resilient plantings. A deep taproot and a wide lateral root system help the tree ride out storms while still pulling moisture from deeper layers of soil.

As fruits ripen, the skin can soften and show gentle ‚Äúsag lines‚Äù as the pulp inside becomes syrupy sweet ‚Äì a small visual signal that the MayaNut superfood season has arrived.

Agroforestry & Superfood Systems

In traditional farming landscapes, Brosimum alicastrum often appears as a scattered shade tree over crops or grazing. It can share space with cacao, coffee, fruit trees, or pasture, providing shade, leaf litter, and long-term structure.

For modern agroforestry, The MayaNut Company views the species as a superfood anchor tree. It can form the canopy layer in mixed plantings where understory crops change over time, but the MayaNut trees remain, storing carbon and producing seeds for beverages and flours. This combination of food value and ecosystem service is the heart of our long-term vision.

Ecology & Forest Role

Ecologically, MayaNut trees are hubs of activity. Flowers feed insects; fruits feed birds, mammals, and ground-dwelling wildlife; and the dense canopy creates a cool, moist microclimate beneath the tree. Fallen leaves add organic matter to the soil, helping retain moisture and nutrients.

In recovering landscapes, planting Brosimum alicastrum can speed up the return of shade and structure. Because people also value the seeds, there is a strong incentive to keep the canopy standing instead of cutting it. That is exactly the kind of alignment between human needs and forest health we want to promote.

Domestication & Genetic Diversity

Globally, Brosimum alicastrum is still in an early stage of domestication. Local communities may recognize individual ‚Äúgood trees‚Äù ‚Äì with better flavor or more fruit ‚Äì but there has not yet been a large, coordinated effort to create named cultivars the way there is for crops like mango or citrus.

The MayaNut Company is intentionally collecting seeds from as many different sources as possible. The species includes at least two recognized subspecies ‚Äì subsp. alicastrum and subsp. bolivarense ‚Äì and our goal is to keep that genetic tapestry intact. A diverse seed base means future plantations are more resilient to pests, diseases, and climate shifts.

Reforestation & Our Survival Directive

Every MayaNut tree we plant is part of a larger directive: ensure that Brosimum alicastrum survives and thrives at both the genetic and academic level. That means documenting where seeds come from, how trees perform, and what traits they show ‚Äì and sharing that knowledge over time.

As a long-lived, food-producing, carbon-storing canopy tree, MayaNut is an ideal candidate for climate-positive reforestation. Our plantations and trials are designed not just to produce MayaNut superfood products, but to serve as living reference collections that keep the species visible to researchers, growers, and future generations.

This website exists partly as a public notebook: a place where data, observations, and real-world experience with Brosimum alicastrum can be recorded, refined, and built upon for decades to come.

RECIPES ‚Äì COOKING WITH MAYANUT SUPERFOOD

Recipes You Can Make With MayaNut

MayaNut is a flexible superfood ingredient. The roasted seed of Brosimum alicastrum can be ground into a powder that behaves a lot like cocoa or roasted grain flour. Below are simple, original recipe ideas built around future MayaNut powder and fresh fruit.

These recipes are for inspiration only until our own MayaNut products are commercially available.

1. Simple MayaNut Superfood Beverage

Ingredients (1‚Äì2 servings)

  • 2‚Äì3 teaspoons MayaNut powder
  • 8‚Äì10 oz hot water or milk of your choice
  • 1‚Äì2 teaspoons sugar, honey, or sweetener (to taste)
  • Pinch of cinnamon or vanilla extract (optional)

Method

  • Warm your water or milk until steaming but not boiling.
  • Whisk in MayaNut powder until smooth and dissolved.
  • Add sweetener and a pinch of cinnamon or vanilla, then stir again.
  • Serve hot, or chill and pour over ice for a cold MayaNut drink.

2. MayaNut ‚ÄúChocolate-Style‚Äù Drink

Ingredients

  • 2 teaspoons MayaNut powder
  • 1 teaspoon cocoa powder
  • 8 oz milk or milk alternative
  • 1‚Äì2 teaspoons sugar or syrup
  • Small pinch of salt

Method

  • Heat the milk gently in a small pot.
  • Whisk together MayaNut powder and cocoa with a few tablespoons of warm milk.
  • Pour this paste back into the pot and stir until smooth.
  • Add sugar and a pinch of salt, then serve warm.

3. MayaNut Tortillas / Flatbread

Ingredients

  • 1 cup wheat or corn flour
  • 1/4‚Äì1/3 cup MayaNut powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • Warm water (just enough to form a soft dough)

Method

  • Mix flour, MayaNut powder, and salt in a bowl.
  • Add warm water a little at a time until a soft dough forms.
  • Rest the dough for 10‚Äì15 minutes, then divide into small balls.
  • Press or roll each ball into a thin circle.
  • Cook on a hot, dry pan until lightly browned on each side.

4. MayaNut Superfood Cookies

Ingredients

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour
  • 1/2 cup MayaNut powder
  • 1/2 cup sugar
  • 1/2 cup butter or oil
  • 1 egg (or egg substitute)
  • 1/2 teaspoon baking powder
  • A pinch of salt and vanilla extract

Method

  • Cream sugar with butter or oil, then mix in the egg and vanilla.
  • In another bowl, whisk flour, MayaNut powder, baking powder, and salt.
  • Combine wet and dry ingredients to form a soft dough.
  • Scoop small portions onto a baking sheet.
  • Bake at about 350¬∞F (175¬∞C) until edges are lightly browned.

5. MayaNut Fruit Jam (From Fresh Fruit)

Ingredients

  • Fresh, fully ripe MayaNut fruits (pulp removed from seeds)
  • Sugar (approximately 60‚Äì70% of the weight of the pulp)
  • Small splash of lemon or lime juice

Method

  • Remove seeds and any tough parts from the fruit, keeping the soft, syrupy pulp.
  • Weigh the pulp, then add sugar equal to about two-thirds of that weight.
  • Cook gently in a pan, stirring often, until the mixture thickens.
  • Add a splash of lemon or lime juice for brightness.
  • Cool and store in clean jars in the refrigerator for short-term use.

Why August Kokus Chose MayaNut

There are easier ways to start a company than planting trees that might take years to bear. But August Kokus wanted something more permanent ‚Äì a visible, rooted footprint on Earth. MayaNut offered that possibility: a species with deep Indigenous history, almost no mainstream recognition, and a taste profile that fits naturally into drinks, baked goods, and modern superfood culture.

The vision is simple: grow the trees, protect the genetics, and let the products speak for themselves. A MayaNut beverage should taste clean and comforting, with as few ingredients as possible. The label should read like a short sentence, not a paragraph of additives.

2025‚Äì2030: First Plantations

Between now and roughly 2030, the main work is quiet: planting, grafting, observing, and taking notes. How fast do different seed families grow? Which ones branch low, and which shoot straight for the sky? How do roots behave in sandy versus limerock soils?

As those answers arrive, the first commercial plots of MayaNut trees will take shape. They will be the foundation for every future bag of MayaNut powder, every chilled drink, and every wholesale shipment of roasted superfood ingredient that leaves the farm.

Looking Toward 2100

The MayaNut Company is, by design, a long-horizon project. Plantations that begin in the 2020s should still be standing in 2100, casting shade over younger crops and storing carbon in roots that no one sees. By then, we hope the phrase ‚ÄúMayaNut beverage‚Äù is as normal as ‚Äúcoffee‚Äù or ‚Äúhot chocolate‚Äù.

If those future consumers look back and see early field photos, hand-drawn logos, and this first layout of mayanut.com, that is part of the reward: proof that a small idea in Florida grew into a worldwide appreciation for Brosimum alicastrum.

What is MayaNut?

‚ÄúMayaNut‚Äù is our modern name for the edible seed of Brosimum alicastrum, a tropical canopy tree in the Moraceae family. The roasted seed can be ground into a powder for drinks, tortillas, flatbreads, and baked goods.
