About The MayaNut Company

The MayaNut Company was founded in 2025 by August Kokus, who wanted to leave behind something new and unique in the world.

Our focus is Brosimum alicastrum — often called maya nut, ojoche, ramón, or breadnut. It’s native to the tropical forests of Mesoamerica and the Caribbean, from Mexico and Guatemala through Belize and Honduras, into parts of Nicaragua, Costa Rica, and Panama, and across to islands like Cuba and Jamaica, as well as northern regions of Colombia and Venezuela. In many areas, you’ll still find towering mayanut trees near ancient ceremonial sites, where they were likely planted and cared for by Indigenous communities.

History of Brosimum alicastrum

The Brosimum lineage occurs in the Western Hemisphere, closely related to Trymatococcus and Helianthostylis, and shares morphological similarities to Treculia, an African lineage, thus its placement in Dorstenieae. Humans in the Americas must have first encountered Brosimum alicastrum thousands of years before Europeans arrived, with written documentation appearing in the mid-1700s. Early hunter-gatherers and later settled Indigenous groups moving into tropical lowland forests would have noticed its generous seed crops. Over time, the tree became woven into local food traditions. In parts of the Maya region, its seeds were dried, roasted, ground into flour, or cooked into porridges and beverages. During times of drought or crop failure, mayanut helped people bridge the gap between harvests as a famine food. Its presence on islands like Cuba and Jamaica may even reflect intentional human transport of seeds a few thousand years ago.

Today the species is still present in forests and shade agroforestry systems, but it’s underused in mainstream agriculture. We see that as a huge opportunity — both for farmers and for the tree itself.

What We’re Doing in South Florida

At The MayaNut Company, we’re working to establish South Florida’s first dedicated mayanut plantation. We’re testing how the trees handle our soils, rainfall patterns, and hurricanes, and how they can fit into diversified farms alongside fruit trees, pasture, and native plants. Our long-term goal is simple: make mayanut so useful and so beloved that planting it becomes a no-brainer for growers.

Future Products

The seeds of Brosimum alicastrum are incredibly versatile. When roasted and ground, they can produce a rich, coffee-like beverage with their own unique character — naturally caffeine-free, but still full of flavor. We plan to offer ground mayanut that people can brew at home, as well as ready-to-drink bottled mayanut beverages.

Our aim is to begin bringing these products to market — including grocery stores — around 2028. Between now and then, we’re focused on growing trees, refining recipes, and building a supply chain that respects both farmers and forests.

For us, this is more than a product line. It’s a way to give an ancient tree a modern job — one that feeds people, supports reforestation, and quietly carries a human story forward in every seed.
