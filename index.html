ABOUT THE MAYANUT COMPANY

About This Project

The MayaNut Company is a long-horizon effort to turn MayaNut into a modern superfood industry while treating the tree as a living archive. That means field trials, genetic diversity, and honest notes about what the tree can – and cannot – do in different climates.

At its core, this project is about more than a drink. It is about long-lived trees, long-lived forests, and a realistic path from scattered wild or backyard MayaNut trees to well-documented plantings that can support future beverages, powders, and flours.

The site you are reading is intentionally dense: part brand home, part field notebook, part quiet promise that Brosimum alicastrum will not be forgotten in the rush for faster-growing crops. Every trial tree, every seed lot, and every future bottle of MayaNut drink ties back to that directive.

Why South Florida Trials?

Early work focuses on frost-free parts of South Florida, where deep soils, high humidity, and hurricane exposure all test what a MayaNut plantation might look like in the 2030s and beyond.

Field notes here will not replace academic papers, but they will give growers and researchers a grounded sense of how the tree behaves outside its historic range.

RESEARCH – TREE, GENETICS & FIELD NOTES

Research & Long-Term Notes

This section keeps track of how MayaNut is described, where it grows, and how different seed lines behave. It combines classic botany with practical field notes – from nuez de ramón in Mesoamerica to Florida trials.

Common Names & Synonyms

Brosimum alicastrum is known by many common names across its range. In English and Spanish it is often called MayaNut or nuez de ramón. Other names include breadnut, ojoche, ramón, guayumaro, guayumaru, ox / oox, and guaimaro.

These names matter for both history and SEO discoverability, so we repeat them across the site in visible and keyword-pill form.

nuez de ramón ojoche breadnut

Taxonomy & Family

Brosimum alicastrum sits within the Moraceae family – the same family that holds figs (Ficus), mulberries (Morus), and breadfruit (Artocarpus). Within that family, it is part of the Brosimeae clade, a group of trees with dense, nutritious seeds and complex inflorescences.

Long-term aim: maintain a clean, updatable summary of Brosimeae research, from early descriptions through modern DNA work.

Indigenous Use & Food Security

In parts of Mesoamerica, MayaNut has historically been used as a staple or emergency food, roasted and ground into a drink or flour. Stories about “famine food” sometimes underplay its real culinary potential: when handled correctly, the seed can become a base for smooth beverages, porridges, and baked goods.

Long-term goal: document how MayaNut can contribute to climate-resilient diets today, without rewriting the past or overstating claims.

Ecology & Morphology

Brosimum alicastrum is a canopy tree with thick evergreen foliage and fruits that resemble small figs when ripe. Soft, sweet pulp surrounds a starchy seed – the part we roast as MayaNut. Deep roots and a sturdy trunk make the tree relevant for soil stability and long-term carbon storage.

Genetics & Seed Lots

The MayaNut Company treats each seed lot as a data point: collection region, altitude, climate, and how the resulting trees behave over time. Rather than collapse everything into a single “improved” line, the aim is to preserve regional diversity and document observable traits.

Future updates will summarize growth speed, canopy form, and fruiting patterns without pretending to replace formal genetic studies.

Florida Trials

Early plantings in South Florida focus on frost-free sites and soils that drain well but still hold enough moisture for deep roots. Notes from these trials – including hurricane response, flowering times, and cold events – will be added slowly so that growers can learn from real-world data instead of speculation.

What is MayaNut?

“MayaNut” is our modern name for the edible seed of Brosimum alicastrum, often known as nuez de ramón, ojoche, or breadnut. The roasted seed can be milled into a powder for drinks, porridges, tortillas, and baked goods. It is naturally caffeine-free and fits into the world of superfood beverages and clean label ingredients.

Is this the same as ramón / ojoche / guaimaro?

In many cases, yes. Across its native range people say ramón, ojoche, guayumaro, guaimaro, ox / oox, and more. The MayaNut Company uses “MayaNut” as an easy global name, while still honoring these local names and keeping them visible on the site for cultural respect and SEO discoverability.

When will products be for sale?

The first commercial MayaNut powder and ready-to-drink concepts are targeted for the late 2020s. Trees must be planted, grown, and harvested correctly before large-scale production makes sense. When products are ready, the Store section and cart icon will shift from “coming soon” to active ordering.

What is the long-term mission of this website?

mayanut.com is designed to last decades. It exists as a public record of how Brosimum alicastrum performs in different climates, which seed lines were used, and how MayaNut superfood products evolve. The site should stay useful to growers, researchers, and curious eaters, not just to customers.

Short version: grow a superfood industry that also protects the tree’s genetics and field knowledge.

Can I grow MayaNut myself?

In tropical and frost-free subtropical regions, yes – with the usual caveat to check local regulations before importing seeds or plants. Early plantings in South Florida are helping define what is realistic at the edge of the tree’s comfortable climate range.
