About the MayaNut Tree ‚Ä¢ Brosimum alicastrum

A Tropical Superfood Tree With Many Names

Brosimum alicastrum belongs to the mulberry family (Moraceae) and is native to large parts of Mesoamerica and northern South America. Across its range, people know it by many names: maya nut, breadnut, ram√≥n, ramon, ojoche, ox or oox, guayumaro, guayumaro, guaymaro and gu√°imaro. In some regions it is simply grouped with ‚Äúpan de √°rbol‚Äù (bread-tree) types because of its nourishing, bread-like seed.

In Maya languages, names often emphasize the tree‚Äôs importance as a reliable food during difficult years. In Spanish-speaking areas, ‚Äúram√≥n‚Äù and ‚Äúojoche‚Äù appear in everyday conversations among farmers, foragers and elders who remember gathering the seeds or fruit beneath giant trees. English speakers sometimes call it breadnut, highlighting how the roasted seed and flour can be baked into breads, tortillas and cookies.

The brand name MayaNut brings these traditions into a single, simple word that can travel globally ‚Äì while still honoring the original Indigenous uses and knowledge that carried Brosimum alicastrum through centuries.

Indigenous Use & Traditional Knowledge

For generations, communities have used MayaNut seeds as a staple and emergency food. The seeds can be roasted until aromatic and brown, then ground into a powder for hot or cold drinks, or milled into flour for tortillas and flatbreads. Families have eaten the tender leaves and young shoots as leafy greens, and livestock graze under the deep shade of mature trees.

In many stories, MayaNut appears as a quiet safety net: a tree that keeps producing during droughts, hurricanes, and years when other crops fail. That resilience is part of what makes it such a powerful candidate for modern superfood beverages and foods ‚Äì especially in a changing climate.

Why Plant MayaNut Now?

The MayaNut Company focuses on the long game: planting and studying diverse seed sources, including how taproots behave in limestone, how trees handle wind and hurricanes, and what densities work for agroforestry, silvopasture and mixed fruit systems. Every planting is both a future superfood source and a living research plot.

Our goal is to help make Brosimum alicastrum common in the tropics by 2100 ‚Äì as windbreaks, shade trees, food trees, and genetic reservoirs tied to documented provenance. Today‚Äôs work exists so that future growers, scientists, and families can enjoy MayaNut for centuries.

Recipe 1 ‚Äì Mocha-Style MayaNut Superfood Drink

Ingredients (1 large mug)

  • 1‚Äì2 tablespoons MayaNut Powder (roasted beverage grade)
  • 8‚Äì10 oz hot water or milk (dairy or plant-based)
  • 1‚Äì2 teaspoons sweetener of choice (honey, sugar, panela, etc.)
  • Optional: a pinch of cinnamon or vanilla

Instructions

  1. Place MayaNut Powder in a mug and add a small splash of hot liquid.
  2. Stir into a smooth paste to avoid clumps.
  3. Top up with the rest of the hot water or milk while stirring.
  4. Sweeten to taste and add cinnamon or vanilla if desired.

Recipe 2 ‚Äì MayaNut Tortillas or Flatbreads

Ingredients (makes 6‚Äì8 small tortillas)

  • 1 cup maize flour or all-purpose flour
  • 1/2 cup MayaNut Flour (from finely milled roasted MayaNut)
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • ~3/4 cup warm water (adjust as needed)

Instructions

  1. Mix dry ingredients together until evenly combined.
  2. Add warm water gradually, stirring and kneading until a soft dough forms.
  3. Divide into small balls and press or roll into thin rounds.
  4. Cook on a hot dry pan or comal until lightly browned on both sides.

These tortillas or flatbreads add subtle roasted depth and extra nutrients compared with standard flour.

Recipe 3 ‚Äì Simple MayaNut Cookies

Ingredients (approx. 12 cookies)

  • 1/2 cup softened butter or coconut oil
  • 1/2 cup sugar or preferred sweetener
  • 1 egg (or egg substitute)
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla
  • 1/2 cup MayaNut Flour
  • 1 cup all-purpose flour
  • 1/2 teaspoon baking powder
  • Pinch of salt

Instructions

  1. Cream butter and sugar together until light.
  2. Beat in egg and vanilla.
  3. In a separate bowl, whisk MayaNut Flour, all-purpose flour, baking powder and salt.
  4. Combine wet and dry mixtures to form a soft dough.
  5. Scoop small balls onto a baking sheet and flatten slightly.
  6. Bake at 350¬∞F (175¬∞C) for about 10‚Äì12 minutes, until edges are lightly golden.

The MayaNut Flour adds a gentle roasted flavor that pairs well with chocolate chips, nuts or dried fruit if you choose to experiment.

Recipe 4 ‚Äì Preserved MayaNut Fruit Jam (Concept)

  • 3‚Äì4 cups ripe MayaNut fruit pulp (without seeds)

  • 1‚Äì2 tablespoons lemon or lime juice

  2. Combine pulp, sugar and citrus juice in a heavy pot.

Research ‚Ä¢ Genetics, Spacing & Survival

Common Names, Subspecies & Genetic Questions

Underneath all the common names ‚Äì maya nut, breadnut, ram√≥n, ojoche, guaimaro and more ‚Äì is Brosimum alicastrum, a species with interesting geographic variation. Some botanists recognize two subspecies, others emphasize local ecotypes adapted to limestone hills, deep soils, or seasonally flooded lowlands.

The MayaNut Company is interested in how these names, stories and landscapes connect back to genetics. Over time, we aim to document where different seed lots originate, how they behave in South Florida and other new climates, and which sources perform best for fruiting, root structure and long-term survival.

Spacing, Taproots & Limestone Experiments

One focus area is understanding how MayaNut behaves when planted in rows versus grids, and how closely trees can be planted before canopy competition becomes a problem. Trials test spacing from tight experimental plots to wider agroforestry and silvopasture systems.

Another theme is taproot behavior: how far does the taproot explore shallow limestone, how does it respond to rocks and voids, and what happens after pruning or transplanting? Observations of root systems ‚Äì from young seedlings to older trees ‚Äì help inform best practices for nurseries, transplanting and long-term anchoring in windy sites.

Why This Research Exists

The entire site, including the store and recipes, rests on a simple mission: help ensure that Brosimum alicastrum survives and thrives academically and genetically. That means linking living trees to data about where they came from, how they grow, and what they produce ‚Äì not just selling a product.

By sharing what we learn about seed sources, climate boundaries, hurricane behavior and long-term performance, we hope to make it easier for future growers, scientists and communities to plant MayaNut with confidence.

FAQ ‚Ä¢ QUESTIONS ABOUT MAYANUT & BROSIMUM ALICASTRUM

Frequently Asked Questions

What does MayaNut fruit taste like?

Ripe MayaNut fruit is syrupy sweet and chewy ‚Äì often compared to raisins and figs, but juicier than a fig with a slight tanginess. The skin can show fine ‚Äúsag lines‚Äù when overripe. Inside, the pulp surrounds a seed that becomes the roasted MayaNut used for beverages and flour.

Unripe fruit, by contrast, can taste woody and astringent. For jams or fresh eating, fully ripe fruit is ideal.

Is MayaNut really a superfood?

‚ÄúSuperfood‚Äù is not a strict scientific category, but Brosimum alicastrum fits many of the traits people associate with superfoods: a resilient tree that provides a versatile seed for beverages, flour and baked goods, plus fruit that can be preserved as jam. It also plays a role in reforestation and carbon storage, making it a superfood for landscapes as well as people.

How do the roots of Brosimum alicastrum behave?

MayaNut is known for strong root systems with taproots that explore deeply where soils allow. In limestone areas, roots can cling to rock pockets and voids, helping anchor the tree against high winds. That combination of deep exploration and lateral anchoring is one reason Brosimum alicastrum is being studied as a hurricane-resistant shade and superfood tree.

What is this website‚Äôs deeper mission?

Beyond future beverages and merchandise, this site exists to help ensure the survival of Brosimum alicastrum on an academic and genetic level. That means:

  • Documenting seed sources and field performance.
  • Sharing practical notes about roots, wind, spacing and climate limits.
  • Highlighting the cultural history of MayaNut under names like ram√≥n, ojoche and guaimaro.

The more people understand and plant this tree, the safer its genetic diversity becomes.

Where can MayaNut grow successfully?

In its native range, MayaNut grows from southern Mexico through Central America into parts of northern South America. In Florida, early observations suggest that areas at or below roughly 27¬∞N (just south of Stuart, Florida) may be safest for long-term success, with possible survival a bit farther north where winters are mild. More data from trials will refine that picture.

How will products be sold and shipped?

Once plantations reach reliable production, The MayaNut Company intends to focus on wholesale: roasted MayaNut powder, beverage bases and flour that can be used by caf√©s, bakeries, food producers and co-brand partners. Select branded drinks and snacks may also be offered directly.

How does this connect to reforestation and carbon?

Every long-lived MayaNut tree is a container of carbon ‚Äì in its wood, roots and surrounding soil. By designing plantings that combine superfood production with shade, habitat and soil protection, MayaNut becomes a tool for both climate resilience and food security.
