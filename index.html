About The MayaNut Company

The MayaNut Company exists to treat MayaNut as both a future superfood ingredient and a long-lived canopy tree that deserves serious, careful cultivation ‚Äì in its native range and in places like South Florida where conditions allow.

The Tree: Brosimum alicastrum

Brosimum alicastrum is an evergreen Moraceae tree that can reach 20‚Äì40 meters in height. It carries dense foliage, deep roots, and fruits whose sweet pulp surrounds a starchy, edible seed ‚Äì what we call MayaNut.

The tree is built for shade, rain, and long-term presence in tropical and subtropical landscapes.

The Company Directive

The directive is simple and long-term: turn MayaNut into a meaningful superfood industry while protecting the species at a genetic and academic level. That includes tracking seed sources, documenting performance, and sharing what we learn with growers and researchers.

A commercial brand that also behaves like a living archive and reforestation engine.

South Florida Trials

Early plantings in South Florida test how Brosimum alicastrum handles limestone-influenced soils, seasonal dry periods, and hurricanes. These trials are designed to explore spacing, root behavior, and long-term canopy stability.

If the tree proves itself under these conditions, it becomes a strong candidate for larger plantings and future supply of MayaNut seed.

Brosimum alicastrum ‚Äì Research & Superfood Background

The leaves of Brosimum alicastrum tell part of the story: a dense, evergreen canopy built for shade, rain, and long-term resilience in tropical and subtropical landscapes. All of the sections below grow out of that simple fact ‚Äì a robust tree carrying a powerful food seed.

Across its native range, people know this species under many names. On this site we use the unified, modern term MayaNut for the edible seed, while still honoring the local words that kept the tree alive in memory.

Common Names & Indigenous Use

Brosimum alicastrum has many names. In English and Spanish it is often called MayaNut, nuez de ram√≥n, or ojoche. In different regions it may be known as breadnut, ram√≥n, ox / oox, guayumaro, and guaimaro. All of these point back to the same basic idea: a canopy tree whose fruits and seeds can feed people and animals.

For many Indigenous communities, fruits were collected and the seeds roasted, ground, or stored as a reserve food. Roasted seeds can make a warm drink similar to a caffeine-free coffee or cocoa replacement, or be turned into flour for tortillas, breads, and porridges.

Natural Range & Climate

In the wild, Brosimum alicastrum grows from southern Mexico through Central America into parts of northern South America. It appears in lowland and foothill forests, tolerating both humid rainforest conditions and seasonal dry forests where long, dusty seasons alternate with heavy rains.

Once established, the tree can handle dry spells surprisingly well, thanks in part to its root system and evergreen canopy. That flexibility ‚Äì handling both wetter and drier sites ‚Äì is one reason The MayaNut Company sees it as a strong candidate for long-term plantings in South Florida and similar climates.

Agroforestry & Superfood Systems

In traditional landscapes, Brosimum alicastrum often appears as a scattered shade tree over crops or grazing. It can share space with cacao, coffee, fruit trees, or pasture, providing shade, leaf litter, and long-term structure.

For modern agroforestry, The MayaNut Company treats the species as a superfood anchor tree. Understory crops may change over time, but the MayaNut trees remain ‚Äì storing carbon, defining the canopy, and producing seeds for beverages and flours.

Genetics, Diversity & Survival

Globally, Brosimum alicastrum is still in an early stage of domestication. Communities may recognize individual ‚Äúgood trees‚Äù, but there are not yet many named cultivars the way there are for mango or citrus. The species includes at least two subspecies ‚Äì subsp. alicastrum and subsp. bolivarense ‚Äì and keeping that diversity intact matters.

Our directive is to help the species survive at both a genetic and academic level: documenting seed sources, traits, and performance so future growers and researchers can build on a clear record instead of starting from scratch.
