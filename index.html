Common Names & Synonyms

Brosimum alicastrum is known by many common names across its range. In English and Spanish it is often called maya nut or nuez de ram√≥n. Other names include breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, guaimaro, and regional spellings such as guayumaro, oox or ox. In parts of Colombia and Venezuela, local names emphasize its role as a shade and food tree in humid lowland forests, and it may be grouped in everyday speech with other ‚Äúpan de √°rbol‚Äù or bread-tree types.

Botanically, two subspecies are currently recognized: Brosimum alicastrum subsp. alicastrum, more widespread in Mesoamerica, and Brosimum alicastrum subsp. bolivarense, documented further south in parts of northern South America. Both belong to the Moraceae family, which also includes figs, mulberries, breadfruit, and jackfruit.

Natural Range & Climate

In its natural range, Brosimum alicastrum occurs from southern Mexico through Central America into parts of northern South America. It is typically found in lowland to mid-elevation tropical and subtropical forests, where rainfall can range from pronounced dry seasons to year-round humidity depending on the region.

The species shows a surprising ability to handle seasonal drought when well established, while still thriving in wetter rainforest conditions. This flexibility is one of the reasons The MayaNut Company believes it has strong potential in South Florida, where winters are mild and the warm, humid growing season can support vigorous growth.

Growth & Tree Form

MayaNut trees can reach heights of 20‚Äì40 meters in favorable sites, developing a broad, rounded crown with dense foliage. Young trees may put much of their energy into roots and structural wood before showing explosive canopy growth. Over time, the tree can become a majestic shade tree, with a sturdy trunk and buttress roots in older specimens.

Leaves are typically glossy green, and the tree produces small flowers followed by olive-like fruits that contain the edible seed commonly referred to as the ‚Äúnut.‚Äù These seeds can be roasted and ground into a powder for beverages, flour, and other food products.

In Agriculture & Agroforestry

Within traditional farming systems, Brosimum alicastrum often appears in agroforestry settings, providing shade for understory crops while producing edible seeds and valuable biomass. The tree‚Äôs deep roots and evergreen canopy help buffer temperature swings, reduce wind, and protect soil.

For modern agroforestry, MayaNut offers opportunities as both a tree crop and a component of diversified systems with coffee, cacao, fruit trees, or grazing. It can be integrated into silvopasture designs, alley cropping, or mixed-species forest plantings where the long-lived canopy and seed production add resilience.

Ecology & Forest Role

Ecologically, Brosimum alicastrum provides food and shelter for wildlife. Fruits and seeds are eaten by birds, mammals, and insects, helping support local food webs. The dense canopy and large root system contribute to forest structure, soil stabilization, and microclimate regulation.

In degraded landscapes, reintroducing MayaNut can help rebuild canopy complexity and support a wider range of species over time. Its combination of human food value and habitat value makes it a unique bridge between conservation and agriculture.

Future Directions & Innovation

The MayaNut Company is focused on exploring how this underutilized tree can be brought into modern food and beverage culture in a responsible way. That includes experimenting with flavor profiles, roasting levels, and blends to highlight the natural taste of MayaNut in beverages, powders, and baked goods.

At the same time, we are interested in how carefully designed plantations and mixed-species systems can create climate-resilient landscapes that store carbon, protect soil, and support biodiversity ‚Äî all while producing a unique tropical product.

Domestication & Selection

Unlike many major crops that have gone through centuries of intensive breeding, Brosimum alicastrum is still in an early stage of domestication from a global perspective. Local communities have long valued certain trees for taste or productivity, but there has not yet been a large, coordinated effort to select elite cultivars.

The MayaNut Company sees an opportunity to carefully evaluate trees for traits like yield, flavor, bearing age, and climate tolerance. Over time, this can lead to selected lines that perform better in specific regions while still preserving the overall genetic diversity of the species.

Genetic Diversity

Brosimum alicastrum is a common species in semi-dry rainforests and rainforests across Central America into northern parts of South America. We are currently working in Florida to acquire as many different seed sources of Brosimum alicastrum as possible to conserve this species.

Most cultivars in agriculture tend to lack genetic diversity and rely too much on human intervention to succeed. Our goal is to go on expeditions and collaborate with growers to source the best, highest-yielding seed families of Brosimum alicastrum, thus helping protect a species that otherwise could be lost to deforestation and habitat fragmentation.

Though seeds are recalcitrant and have a short shelf life in agriculture, roasted seeds made into powder can last more than a year if handled correctly. The MayaNut Company hopes to acquire more seed sources of Brosimum alicastrum subsp. alicastrum and obtain seeds of Brosimum alicastrum subsp. bolivarense to secure this species‚Äô future.

Reforestation & Carbon Storage

MayaNut is not just a food tree ‚Äî it is also a powerful tool for reforestation and climate resilience. A mature Brosimum alicastrum tree can store significant amounts of carbon in its wood, roots, and surrounding soil, especially when planted in diverse, long-lived forest systems.

Because the tree produces a valuable edible seed, there is a built-in economic incentive to maintain it as living biomass instead of clearing it. This aligns reforestation goals with community livelihoods. In regions where forests have been cut, MayaNut can be part of mixed-species plantings that restore shade, rebuild soil, and create corridors for wildlife while providing long-term harvests for people.

Our vision is for future plantings of Brosimum alicastrum to function as ‚Äúfood forests‚Äù and carbon sinks, combining climate mitigation with real, tangible products ‚Äî including MayaNut beverages and food ingredients ‚Äî that people love.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

What is MayaNut?

MayaNut is the roasted seed of Brosimum alicastrum, a tropical tree native to Mesoamerica and parts of northern South America. It is also known as breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, and guaimaro. The seeds can be roasted and ground into a flavorful powder for beverages, flour, and other foods.

Where does The MayaNut Company grow its trees?

We are establishing plantations in South Florida, where the subtropical climate is well suited for Brosimum alicastrum. Our long-term goal is to create a productive, diverse plantation that showcases how MayaNut can thrive outside its original range while still respecting genetic diversity and conservation.

When will MayaNut beverages be available?

Because we are planting trees and allowing them to mature, this is a long-term project. Our goal is to have a functional plantation by around 2030. As the trees begin producing, we will move from concept beverages to real product development and wholesale offerings.

Is MayaNut related to other familiar trees?

Yes. MayaNut belongs to the Moraceae family, which also includes figs, mulberries, breadfruit, and jackfruit. It shares some traits with these trees, such as its milky sap and its role as a canopy or subcanopy species in tropical forests.

How will your products be sold?

Over time, The MayaNut Company plans to focus on wholesale bulk products: roasted MayaNut powder, flour, and beverage bases that can be used by caf√©s, food producers, and retailers. We also plan to offer branded finished beverages and select merchandise through partners and online channels.

Why focus on genetic diversity?

Many commercial crops depend on a narrow genetic base, which can make them vulnerable to pests, diseases, and climate shifts. For Brosimum alicastrum, we want to conserve and work with as many different seed sources as possible, including both subspecies, so that future plantings are resilient and adaptable.

Can MayaNut help with reforestation and carbon storage?

Yes. As a long-lived canopy tree that produces human food, MayaNut helps align reforestation with livelihoods. When integrated into mixed-species forests and agroforestry systems, it can store carbon, shade the soil, support wildlife, and provide a harvestable product at the same time.
