About The MayaNut Company

The MayaNut Company was founded in 2025 by August Kokus in South Florida with a simple but ambitious idea: take an underappreciated tropical tree and build an entire future industry around it. That tree is Brosimum alicastrum ‚Äî known as maya nut, breadnut, ram√≥n, ojoche, and guaimaro.

Instead of chasing a crowded market, August wanted to create something truly new: plantations of MayaNut trees grown in Florida, producing beverages and foods that barely exist in today‚Äôs mainstream culture.

Founder: August Kokus

August has always been drawn to strange, powerful trees: banyans, figs, and other members of the Moraceae family. Discovering that Brosimum alicastrum produces a seed that can be roasted into a mocha-like drink ‚Äî yet is almost unknown in the United States ‚Äî lit a spark that became The MayaNut Company.

His vision is long-term. By around 2030, August aims to have at least one functional MayaNut plantation, with trees spaced carefully to balance root exploration, canopy overlap, and future mechanical harvesting. Looking ahead to 2100, he imagines a world where MayaNut beverages are as familiar as coffee or cocoa, and where entire food forests of Brosimum alicastrum help stabilize climate and livelihoods.

Why Minimal Ingredients Matter

Our future MayaNut drink is being designed with as few ingredients as possible. The goal is to let the roasted seed speak for itself: subtle chocolate, nutty depth, a hint of caramel, and a natural richness that does not need artificial flavors to shine.

Every prototype recipe starts with three questions: Does this respect the flavor of the tree? Is it honest and simple? Would we be proud to serve this to someone tasting MayaNut for the very first time?

Where We‚Äôre Growing

South Florida‚Äôs subtropical climate is one of the few places in the continental United States where Brosimum alicastrum can be trialed outdoors year-round. Our early plantings experiment with different soils, irrigation systems, and wind exposure to learn how MayaNut performs in a hurricane-prone environment.

Early observations show that the roots clasp into the soil and any available limestone, forming a net of structural support that allows the tree to ride out intense winds with minimal damage. This hurricane-resistant architecture is one reason we see MayaNut as a future backbone species in subtropical agroforestry.

Research on Brosimum alicastrum

Names & Cultural History

Brosimum alicastrum carries many names: maya nut, breadnut, ram√≥n, ojoche, guaimaro, and more. These names echo from Mesoamerican civilizations that relied on the tree for food, fodder, and shade.

Its closest ‚Äúcousins‚Äù include figs and mulberries, but MayaNut stands out because its seeds can be roasted, ground, and stored like a staple food ‚Äî a kind of tropical, tree-based grain.

Fruit & Seed Structure

To the eye, MayaNut fruits resemble small, olive-like drupes. Inside is the seed that becomes the ‚Äúnut.‚Äù The fruit itself can be syrupy sweet and chewy, with textures reminiscent of raisins and figs but juicier and with a gentle tang. When overripe, the skin can sag slightly, forming faint lines that show just how soft and sugary the pulp has become.

Unripe fruit, by contrast, can taste woody or green and is best left to ripen on the tree. Once mature, the seed can be roasted to bring out chocolate, coffee, and toasted cereal notes that make it ideal for beverages and baking.

Roots, Limestone & Hurricanes

One of the most fascinating traits of Brosimum alicastrum is its rooting behavior. When grown over limestone or rocky substrates, the roots clasp and weave into cracks, forming an anchor that holds the tree in place during storms.

Instead of shallow, fragile roots, MayaNut tends to combine a strong central taproot with lateral roots that brace the trunk like guy-wires. This makes it a prime candidate for hurricane-resilient agroforestry in coastal and subtropical regions.

Growth, Canopy & Shade

MayaNut can reach 20‚Äì40 meters in height in its native range, with a dense, rounded crown. Leaves are arranged in an alternating pattern along the branches, typically forming an efficient light-capturing canopy that stays green even through pronounced dry seasons once established.

For plantation design, spacing in the range of 5‚Äì6 meters is being tested to balance canopy overlap, root spread, and eventual harvest logistics. The goal is a system that is both beautiful and practical.

Genetic Diversity & Subspecies

Within Brosimum alicastrum, at least two subspecies have been described, and regional variation is obvious to growers: differences in fruit size, flavor, bearing age, and drought tolerance.

The MayaNut Company is focused on sourcing seeds from as many different provenances as possible, treating each mother tree as a potential ‚Äúline‚Äù that could shine under future climates. Diversity today is resilience tomorrow.

Reforestation & Carbon Storage

As a long-lived canopy tree with edible seeds, MayaNut bridges the gap between conservation and agriculture. Planting it in mixed-species systems creates shade, cools microclimates, and sequesters carbon in wood and root biomass.

We imagine future landscapes where rows and clusters of Brosimum alicastrum double as carbon sinks and food forests, with MayaNut beverages and flours serving as tangible, delicious proof that the trees are worth protecting.

Starting a New Tree Crop in a Hurricane Zone

Planting a new tree crop is easy on paper and slow in reality. Every seed has to germinate, establish, and survive the surprises of weather and soil. In South Florida, that also means meeting hurricanes.

Early plantings of Brosimum alicastrum show promising storm behavior: roots cling to the soil and underlying rock, and the canopy flexes instead of shattering. Watching these trees hold their ground in heavy winds is one of the reasons we‚Äôre committing so strongly to MayaNut as a long-term crop.

Designing a Minimalist MayaNut Beverage

The goal for our flagship MayaNut drink is radical simplicity. We want the ingredient list to read like a sentence, not a paragraph. Water, roasted MayaNut, and a few supporting elements to balance texture and sweetness ‚Äî that is the direction.

Instead of chasing sugar-heavy novelty flavors, we are obsessed with the natural taste of the roasted seed: cocoa-like, slightly nutty, with a warm depth that feels familiar yet new. When our first plantation is producing consistently, every detail from roast profile to grind size will be dialed in to showcase that flavor honestly.

2030 and 2100: Two Different Horizons

By 2030, our realistic goal is a functional MayaNut plantation in Florida ‚Äî at least one solid block of trees demonstrating yield, resilience, and product quality. That is the near horizon.

The 2100 horizon is different. On that timeline, we imagine corridors of Brosimum alicastrum stretching across suitable climates, linked to smallholder farms, cafes, and beverage brands that treat MayaNut as a standard ingredient. If even part of that vision becomes reality, it will be because people decided that beautiful, long-lived trees belong at the center of our future food system.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

What does fresh MayaNut fruit taste like?

Ripe MayaNut fruit is syrupy sweet and chewy, with a texture somewhere between raisins and figs but juicier than a fig. Many people notice a gentle tanginess that keeps it from being cloying. If you pick it too early, though, it can taste woody or green ‚Äî best to wait until it is fully colored and beginning to soften.

What does roasted MayaNut powder taste like?

When the seed is roasted and ground, the flavor shifts toward warm chocolate, toasted cereal, and a hint of coffee without the bitterness. It blends naturally into cozy, mocha-like drinks and can be used in baking as a partial flour replacement.

Where are your trees growing?

Our initial plantings are in South Florida, at the edge of the subtropical zone. We are testing different microclimates and soil types to understand how Brosimum alicastrum responds outside its traditional range while maintaining good growth and fruiting.

Why are the roots important?

MayaNut roots anchor deeply and can grip limestone or rocky layers, creating a living anchor system that resists hurricane winds. This makes the species especially attractive for long-term plantings in storm-prone regions, where shallow-rooted trees can fail catastrophically.

When will products be available?

Tree crops move on tree time. Our focus now is on establishing strong, diverse plantings. As trees reach bearing age later in the 2020s, we will scale up roasting trials, beverage concepts, and wholesale powder offerings. The best way to stay informed is to watch this site as we post updates.

Will you sell seedlings or seeds?

In the future, we plan to explore offering planting material sourced from multiple mother trees. Because Brosimum alicastrum seeds have a short storage life in raw form, any distribution will have to be carefully timed and documented. For now, our priority is building and studying our own plantations.

Can MayaNut help with reforestation and carbon storage?

Yes. Long-lived trees like MayaNut are natural carbon reservoirs. When planted in mixed-species systems, they shade the soil, support wildlife, and store carbon above and below ground while also producing edible seeds. That combination of ecological and economic value is at the heart of our mission.
