RESEARCH

Brosimum alicastrum is a species of tree in the family Moraceae. This species is primarily dominant in the tropical forests of southern Mexico, but it also occurs in more sporadic populations as far north as Tamaulipas, and in western parts of Mexico and Central America, extending into northern South America, as well as parts of the Caribbean, including Cuba and Jamaica. In different regions, it is also known as maya nut, breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, or guiamaro.

More research is needed on this species in localized populations. The MayaNut Company is actively working on acquiring new Brosimum alicastrum seeds from different genetic sources to ensure enough genetic diversity for this species to continue surviving effectively in agriculture.

Domestication by the Mayan culture is limited, and the species Brosimum alicastrum could be selectively bred into more stable strains, yielding larger and more nutritious seeds.

Historically, Brosimum alicastrum is believed to have been an important part of not just the Maya people, but probably many other cultures overlapping its range. The tree was likely used by early Mesoamerican hunter-gatherers as early as 10,000 B.C. More recently in history, however, there is very little, if any, direct evidence of its specific uses in ancient cultures. No known texts or art clearly describe recipes or uses in their culture.

Today, maya nut is used as a fodder tree for ranchers in Mexico and to make tortillas, bread, and even a coffee-like beverage, all made from the seeds of this ancient tropical rainforest tree. The MayaNut Company is working in South Florida to establish a dedicated Brosimum alicastrum farm, often called a maya nut plantation, as this species is widely underutilized in modern agroforestry systems.
