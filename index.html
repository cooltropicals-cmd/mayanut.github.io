ABOUT THE MAYANUT COMPANY

About The MayaNut Company

The MayaNut Company is a long-term project built around Brosimum alicastrum, a canopy tree with an edible, roasted seed commonly called MayaNut. This site documents the tree, the genetics, and the slow, careful work of turning an underused species into a future superfood industry.

At its core, The MayaNut Company is about planting time into the landscape: trees that can live for decades, hold soil in place, and provide a starchy, roastable seed. Instead of chasing short-term trends, the project focuses on long-lived agroforestry systems that pair MayaNut with other compatible crops.

Every seed lot is tracked by origin region, elevation, and basic climate. Over the coming years, this will evolve into a living map of how different Brosimum alicastrum lines behave in Florida and across the broader subtropics. The goal is not a single ‚Äúperfect‚Äù tree, but a diverse library of useful genetics.

Long view: a network of growers and researchers using mayanut.com as a shared home base for data, field stories, and eventually ‚Äì real products.

Research & Field Notes

Common Names & Subspecies

Brosimum alicastrum is known by many common names across its range. In English and Spanish it is often called maya nut or nuez de ram√≥n. Other names include breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, guaimaro, and regional spellings such as guayumaro, oox or ox. In parts of Colombia and Venezuela, local names emphasize its role as a shade and food tree in humid lowland forests and may group it informally with other ‚Äúpan de √°rbol‚Äù or bread-tree types.

Botanically, current literature recognizes at least two subspecies: Brosimum alicastrum subsp. alicastrum and B. alicastrum subsp. bolivarense. Their precise geographic and morphological boundaries deserve more attention, especially as new phylogenetic work refines relationships within the Brosimeae.

Indigenous Use & Food Security

In parts of Mesoamerica, MayaNut has historically been used as a staple or emergency food, roasted and ground into a drink or flour. Stories about ‚Äúfamine food‚Äù sometimes underplay its real culinary potential: when handled correctly, the seed can become a base for smooth beverages, porridges, and baked goods. Our research aims to connect these traditional uses to modern processing methods and flavor profiles.

Long-term goal: document how MayaNut can contribute to climate-resilient diets today, without rewriting the past or overstating claims.

Ecology & Morphology

Brosimum alicastrum is a canopy tree in the Moraceae family, with thick evergreen foliage and fruits that resemble small figs when ripe. The soft pulp surrounds a starchy seed. As part of the broader Moraceae lineage, the tree is related to figs, mulberries, and breadfruit, but it occupies its own niche in lowland and mid-elevation tropical forests where it stabilizes soils and stores carbon.

Genetics & Seed Lots

The MayaNut Company treats each seed lot as a data point: where it was collected, what altitude, what climate, and how the resulting trees behave over time. The long view is to create living collections that preserve regional lines, rather than collapsing everything into a single anonymous ‚Äúimproved‚Äù type.

Future updates will summarize observable traits ‚Äì growth speed, canopy form, fruiting patterns ‚Äì without pretending to replace formal genetic studies.

Florida Trials

Early plantings in South Florida focus on frost-free sites and soils that drain well but retain enough moisture for deep-rooted trees. Notes from these trials ‚Äì including hurricane response, flowering times, and cold tolerance ‚Äì will appear here over the next decade so growers can learn from real-world data instead of speculation.

As the project matures, this section will point to formal write-ups, grower guides, and collaborations with universities or botanical gardens. What is MayaNut?

MayaNut is the edible roasted seed of Brosimum alicastrum, a long-lived canopy tree in the Moraceae family. Ground powder works in drinks, tortillas, baked goods, and porridge.

Is this the same as ram√≥n / ojoche?

Yes ‚Äî these are traditional names across Mesoamerica: ram√≥n, ojoche, guayumaro, guaimaro, and more. ‚ÄúMayaNut‚Äù is our simple global name for the same tree.

When will products be available?

Commercial MayaNut powder and drinks are planned for the late 2020s. Trees must be planted, grown, and harvested at scale before real inventory exists.

What is the goal of this website?

This site acts as a public archive for genetics, field notes, and research ‚Äî helping ensure long-term survival and proper documentation of Brosimum alicastrum.

The company is both a future superfood brand and a living research library.

Can I grow MayaNut?

Yes, in frost-free tropical or subtropical climates. Cold below freezing can damage seedlings, but mature trees are tougher. Local regulations vary by region.
