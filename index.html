Brosimum alicastrum ‚Äì Research & Superfood Background

Common Names & Indigenous Use

Brosimum alicastrum has many names. In English and Spanish it is often called MayaNut, nuez de ram√≥n, or ojoche. In different regions it may be known as breadnut, ram√≥n, ox or oox, guayumaro, and guaimaro. All of these point back to the same basic idea: a canopy tree whose fruits and seeds can feed people and animals.

For many Indigenous communities, the tree has been more than a curiosity. Fruits were collected and the seeds roasted, ground, or stored as a reserve food. Roasted seeds can make a warm drink similar to a caffeine-free coffee or cocoa replacement, or they can be turned into flour for tortillas, breads, and porridges. In some areas, families relied on ram√≥n or ojoche as a famine-resilient food when other crops failed.

The MayaNut Company uses the simple, modern name ‚ÄúMayaNut‚Äù as a unifying term for this superfood, while honoring the many local names that kept the tree alive in cultural memory.

Natural Range & Climate

In the wild, Brosimum alicastrum grows from southern Mexico through Central America into parts of northern South America. It appears in lowland and foothill forests, tolerating both humid rainforest conditions and seasonal dry forests where long, dusty seasons alternate with heavy rains.

Once established, the tree can handle dry spells surprisingly well, thanks in part to its deep root system. At the same time it thrives in wetter climates, making it an excellent candidate for tropical and subtropical agroforestry. This flexibility is one reason The MayaNut Company is confident about its future in South Florida.

Growth, Roots & Hurricane Resistance

MayaNut trees can reach 20‚Äì40 meters tall in favorable conditions, with a broad, rounded crown and dense evergreen foliage. Young trees often invest heavily in roots and trunk before showing dramatic canopy expansion. Over time they become serious shade trees, with thick, interlocking roots and, in some sites, buttresses around the base.

Those roots matter: they tend to cling tightly to rocky or limestone-influenced soils, forming a kind of living anchor. That is one of the reasons we see Brosimum alicastrum as an excellent candidate for hurricane-resilient plantings. A deep taproot and a wide lateral root system help the tree ride out storms while still pulling moisture from deeper layers of soil.

As fruits ripen, the skin can soften and show gentle ‚Äúsag lines‚Äù as the pulp inside becomes syrupy sweet ‚Äì a small visual signal that the MayaNut superfood season has arrived.

Agroforestry & Superfood Systems

In traditional farming landscapes, Brosimum alicastrum often appears as a scattered shade tree over crops or grazing. It can share space with cacao, coffee, fruit trees, or pasture, providing shade, leaf litter, and long-term structure.

For modern agroforestry, The MayaNut Company views the species as a superfood anchor tree. It can form the canopy layer in mixed plantings where understory crops change over time, but the MayaNut trees remain, storing carbon and producing seeds for beverages and flours. This combination of food value and ecosystem service is the heart of our long-term vision.

Ecology & Forest Role

Ecologically, MayaNut trees are hubs of activity. Flowers feed insects; fruits feed birds, mammals, and ground-dwelling wildlife; and the dense canopy creates a cool, moist microclimate beneath the tree. Fallen leaves add organic matter to the soil, helping retain moisture and nutrients.

In recovering landscapes, planting Brosimum alicastrum can speed up the return of shade and structure. Because people also value the seeds, there is a strong incentive to keep the canopy standing instead of cutting it. That is exactly the kind of alignment between human needs and forest health we want to promote.

Domestication & Genetic Diversity

Globally, Brosimum alicastrum is still in an early stage of domestication. Local communities may recognize individual ‚Äúgood trees‚Äù ‚Äì with better flavor or more fruit ‚Äì but there has not yet been a large, coordinated effort to create named cultivars the way there is for crops like mango or citrus.

The MayaNut Company is intentionally collecting seeds from as many different sources as possible. The species includes at least two recognized subspecies ‚Äì subsp. alicastrum and subsp. bolivarense ‚Äì and our goal is to keep that genetic tapestry intact. A diverse seed base means future plantations are more resilient to pests, diseases, and climate shifts.

Reforestation & Our Survival Directive

Every MayaNut tree we plant is part of a larger directive: ensure that Brosimum alicastrum survives and thrives at both the genetic and academic level. That means documenting where seeds come from, how trees perform, and what traits they show ‚Äì and sharing that knowledge over time.

As a long-lived, food-producing, carbon-storing canopy tree, MayaNut is an ideal candidate for climate-positive reforestation. Our plantations and trials are designed not just to produce MayaNut superfood products, but to serve as living reference collections that keep the species visible to researchers, growers, and future generations.

This website exists partly as a public notebook: a place where data, observations, and real-world experience with Brosimum alicastrum can be recorded, refined, and built upon for decades to come.
