Common Names & Indigenous Uses

Brosimum alicastrum carries many names across Mesoamerica and northern South America. In English and Spanish it is often called MayaNut, Maya nut, or nuez de ram√≥n. In older literature it appears as breadnut. In Central American Spanish you may see ram√≥n, ojoche, or nuez de ojoche. In parts of Colombia and Venezuela the tree is known as guaimaro or with closely related spellings like guayumaro. In some Mayan languages, short words like ox or oox are used.

These names do more than label a seed; they point back to histories of use. In different Indigenous and rural communities, the tree has served as a shade tree, emergency food, snack, fodder, and quiet part of the daily landscape. In some regions the seeds were dried, ground, or roasted and mixed with maize or other staples. In others, the sweet outer pulp around the seed was eaten fresh from the tree by people, birds, and animals.

Modern interest in ‚ÄúMayaNut‚Äù is often framed around roasted seed powder as a caffeine-free drink base or flour. That is only one chapter. Long before product names existed, families knew individual trees: which one produced earlier in the rainy season, which dropped heavier crops, and which had pulp that was more pleasant to eat fresh. Part of our work is to respect those older observations while exploring new uses such as beverages, baked goods, and quiet staple ingredients.

We use the term MayaNut on this site because it is widely recognized in English-language conversations and helps people find information quickly. However, we also keep the other names visible ‚Äì ram√≥n, ojoche, guaimaro, breadnut ‚Äì as a reminder that this species lives in many languages, not just one.

Tree Form, Genetics & Future Trials

From a distance, Brosimum alicastrum can look like a classic broad-crowned tropical tree. Up close, individual trees differ in branching, leaf size, fruiting behavior, and how they react to wind or cool snaps. For long-term plantings, those differences matter as much as any final beverage recipe.

Our approach is to treat every seed lot as a tiny genetic story. When we plant several dozen seeds from one mother tree and compare them to seeds from another region, we record survival, vigor, and fruiting notes. Over time, those observations may suggest which combinations are better for coastal humidity, inland heat, or the occasional rare cold event in Florida.

None of this is a formal breeding program. It is simply a long-term habit of noticing: which seedlings bend without breaking in storms, which recover fastest after stress, and how early different lines begin to produce usable seed crops. The aim is to quietly build a living library of Brosimum alicastrum trees with known histories.

As this work grows, we plan to share non-technical summaries here for landowners, students, and anyone considering planting MayaNut alongside other perennial crops.

RECIPES ‚Äì USING MAYANUT AT HOME

Recipes You Can Make With MayaNut

These recipes are written around the idea of a simple, honest ingredient list. Most of them use MayaNut powder (roasted Brosimum alicastrum seed), plus basic pantry items. As our own beverage and flour products develop, we will keep refining these ideas ‚Äì but this is a starting point you can build on at home.

MayaNut powder is the core ingredient in most of our recipes ‚Äì a roasted seed flour that can lean toward coffee, cocoa, or toasted cereal notes depending on how it is prepared.
The fresh fruit around the seed is syrupy, chewy, and gently tangy when ripe. One of our goals is to capture that flavor in jams, toppings, and snacks without losing its character.

1. Simple Chilled MayaNut Beverage

Base: MayaNut powder beverage

Stir 1‚Äì2 tablespoons of finely ground MayaNut powder into hot water and let it steep like a caffeine-free coffee. Sweeten lightly, then pour over ice and finish with a splash of milk or a plant-based alternative. The flavor sits somewhere between toasted cereal, mild coffee, and a hint of cocoa ‚Äì simple, comforting, and easy to adjust.

As our own beverage base is finalized, we will publish exact ratios for pre-mixed MayaNut concentrates and ready-to-drink formats.

2. MayaNut ‚ÄúChocolate‚Äù Style Drink

Idea: cocoa & MayaNut together

Combine MayaNut powder with a smaller amount of cocoa powder, then sweeten to taste and whisk into hot milk. The cocoa sharpness rides on top, while MayaNut fills in the background with a deeper, toasted warmth. Served hot, it feels like a gentler cousin of hot chocolate; chilled over ice, it becomes a smooth mocha-like drink without relying purely on coffee.

This is the concept behind some of our future ‚ÄúMayaNut chocolate‚Äù style blends ‚Äì fewer ingredients, with the tree doing most of the flavor work.

3. Soft MayaNut Tortillas

Use: partial flour replacement

Blend a modest portion of MayaNut powder into your usual corn or wheat tortilla dough ‚Äì not as the only flour, but as a flavor-boosting addition. A small percentage is enough to add a toasted, nutlike aroma and a faint caramel color. Cook the tortillas on a hot, dry surface as usual. They pair especially well with simple fillings where the warm, earthy notes can shine.

We recommend starting with a low percentage of MayaNut powder and increasing slowly; too much can change texture. This is the kind of experiment we will keep publishing as our own plantations produce more flour.

4. MayaNut Cookies or Breakfast Bread

Baking: gentle flavor, not overpowering

In a standard cookie or quick-bread recipe, swap part of the flour for MayaNut powder. The result is a softer, deeper flavor that sits somewhere between toasted oats, light coffee, and a hint of malt. Paired with vanilla, cinnamon, or a small amount of cocoa, the cookies take on a ‚Äúcomfort dessert‚Äù profile that can be made with simple ingredients and no artificial flavors.

The MayaNut Company‚Äôs long-term goal is to release simple mixes built around this idea: very few ingredients, where roasted MayaNut does most of the talking.

5. MayaNut Fruit Jam (From Fresh Fruit)

Fruit use: syrupy, chewy & tangy

When the fresh MayaNut fruits are fully ripe, the outer layer becomes syrupy, chewy, and lightly tangy ‚Äì a bit like raisins and figs with more juice. To make a simple jam concept, the pulp is carefully separated from the seeds, gently cooked with a small amount of sugar and a splash of citrus, and then reduced until it holds its shape on a spoon.

The goal is not to erase the natural character of the fruit, but to concentrate it. A future MayaNut fruit jam could be used as a swirl in yogurt, a topping for toast, or even as a thin layer in baked desserts that already use MayaNut flour or powder.

This jam idea is still in the experimental phase. As the first Florida trees bear consistent crops of fruit, we plan to refine recipes that respect the natural sweetness and texture rather than hiding it.

Why August Kokus Chose MayaNut

August Kokus has always been drawn to plants that feel a little bit overlooked ‚Äì the ones that quietly hold entire ecosystems together while rarely appearing on packaging or in grocery aisles. When he found Brosimum alicastrum, he kept returning to the same thought: if one tree could feed people, anchor soil, shade animals, and become a new flavor in everyday life, it was worth dedicating years to.

The MayaNut Company was born from that idea. Not to chase every possible product, but to refine a small set of things done well: beverages with very few ingredients, honest flours, and recipes that let the roasted seed speak for itself. The goal is to make something that can quietly exist for the next century or more ‚Äì a permanent part of how people eat, plant, and think about trees.

Our Drink Philosophy: Fewer Ingredients, More Tree

Many modern drinks hide behind long ingredient lists. Our vision for a MayaNut beverage is the opposite: keep it simple enough that you can describe it in one breath. Roasted MayaNut, water, a touch of sweetness, and maybe a small amount of cocoa or milk if needed. The texture and most of the flavor should come from the tree itself.

That is why so much of our energy goes into field work and genetics instead of packaging. The better the trees, the better the roasted seed, and the fewer extra flavors we need to add. Every future product is a kind of ‚Äúthank you‚Äù to the plantations that made it possible.

FAQ ‚Äì QUESTIONS ABOUT MAYANUT & THIS PROJECT

Frequently Asked Questions

These answers are focused on Brosimum alicastrum (MayaNut / breadnut) and on why this site exists. The goal is not only to create beverages and flours, but to help keep the species alive, studied, and planted at both an academic and genetic level ‚Äì long after current trends have changed.

What exactly is MayaNut?

MayaNut is the roasted seed of the tropical tree Brosimum alicastrum, a member of the Moraceae (mulberry / fig) family. The tree produces a chewy, syrupy fruit around a starchy seed. When the seed is roasted and milled, it becomes a powder that can be brewed as a drink or used as part of a flour blend.

How does it taste?

The roasted seed tends to sit somewhere between toasted cereal, mild coffee, and cocoa. With lighter roasting, it can feel more like warm cereal or a gentle malt. Darker roasting brings in notes closer to coffee or chocolate. One of our main goals is to develop roasting profiles that are repeatable and easy to use in beverages, bakery items, and simple mixes.

Why so much focus on genetics and long-term survival?

Many crops are treated as anonymous commodities. In contrast, this site exists to help keep track of the story of Brosimum alicastrum ‚Äì which seed families are planted, how they perform, and how they might be used in the future. The more we understand and document, the easier it becomes for future growers and researchers to keep the species planted and genetically diverse.

Can MayaNut grow in rocky or limestone soils?

The species is known to grow and send roots down through rocky and limestone-influenced soils in many parts of its native range. One reason it interests us is that it appears capable of anchoring itself even when topsoil is thin. A deep, persistent taproot is part of what we hope will make it suitable for long term, hurricane-aware plantings.

Is this a coffee replacement, a cocoa product, or something else?

It can touch all three spaces without being identical to any of them. Some people may use MayaNut as a caffeine-free ‚Äúcoffee style‚Äù drink. Others may prefer it blended with cocoa for a smoother kind of chocolate drink or used in bakery items as a gentle flavor in the background. The important part is that the tree itself does most of the flavor work.

The store, cart, and product search on this page are here to help us design the experience early, even while the trees are still growing.

No. Nothing on this site is medical advice or a health claim. As the project matures, we may link to research about nutritional profiles, but any questions about diet, allergies, or health still belong with licensed professionals.
