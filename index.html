The MayaNut Company

A modern brand built around an ancient tropical tree.

The MayaNut Company exists to focus on a single species: MayaNut, the edible seed of Brosimum alicastrum. Instead of spreading attention across dozens of ingredients, this project concentrates on one long-lived tree crop and the future foods it can support.

Over time, The MayaNut Company intends to become both a superfood brand and a research-friendly hub for growers, food makers, and scientists who are interested in this species. Commercial products will only roll out when there is a secure, traceable supply of high-quality MayaNut from multiple regions.

In short: build a beautiful brand, document the tree honestly, and treat every planting as part of a long-term archive for Brosimum alicastrum.

The Tree ‚Äì Brosimum alicastrum

Brosimum alicastrum is an evergreen tree in the Moraceae family that can reach 20‚Äì40 meters in height. It produces sweet fruits around a starchy, edible seed that can be roasted and ground into a flour-like powder. Across Mesoamerica and parts of northern South America, this species is known by names such as ram√≥n, ojoche, nuez de ram√≥n, breadnut, guayumaro, guaimaro, and others.

Historically, the tree has been used for shade, timber, and local food. Modern interest is shifting toward its potential as a climate-resilient tree crop for agroforestry systems, carbon storage, and diversified tropical food security. MayaNut can be integrated with other perennial crops, creating multi-layered agroecosystems instead of single-crop fields.

About the Founder ‚Äì August Kokus

The MayaNut Company is led by August Kokus, who has been studying Brosimum alicastrum, its history, and its agricultural potential for many years. August believes that the beauty and potential of life should never be wasted. This work aims to bring the MayaNut tree out of obscurity and into the global spotlight, offering a climate-resilient crop with real potential to improve food security for millions across the tropical world. RESEARCH ‚Äì BOTANY, GENETICS & FUTURE PLANTINGS

Common Names & Indigenous Use

Brosimum alicastrum is known across its range by dozens of names: ram√≥n, ojoche, guayumaro, ox / oox, guaimaro, pan de √°rbol, and breadnut. Many Maya communities historically used the seed as a staple food, drying or roasting it to produce flour for drinks, porridges, or tortillas. In certain regions, fermented MayaNut drinks were a seasonal tradition, connecting the harvest to cultural celebrations.

Archaeologists have found large densities of MayaNut seeds near ancient Maya settlements, suggesting intentional planting or management of the species. In some oral histories, MayaNut is remembered as a famine food, while in others it is valued for its storage qualities, able to last months when dried properly.

Botany & Genetics

Genetic studies show that Brosimum alicastrum forms regional clusters across Mesoamerica and northern South America. Seeds collected from different regions show variation in shape, size, shell thickness, and germination speed. Understanding this variation is important for long-term domestication and conservation efforts.

Leaf shape, internode spacing, and fruiting behavior may also vary by region. Documenting these traits over decades will help identify which lines perform best in South Florida, the Caribbean, or other subtropical zones outside the tree‚Äôs traditional range.

Ecology & Climate Resilience

MayaNut thrives in warm, humid climates with minimal frost. The tree tolerates seasonal droughts, hurricanes, and strong winds thanks to its deep roots and dense wood. In agroforestry systems, it pairs well with cacao, breadfruit, guava, banana, and perennial ground covers.

As climate change shifts rainfall patterns and increases extreme weather events, perennial tree crops like MayaNut may become increasingly essential for maintaining regional food supplies. Its canopy structure also supports biodiversity by providing habitat for birds, insects, and shade-loving understory crops.

Planting & Field Notes

In field tests, young MayaNut trees grow best with consistent moisture, organic mulch, and partial shade during the first year. Spacing of 5‚Äì6 meters (~18 ft) is recommended to allow canopy development. Mixed plantings with nitrogen fixers may improve early growth.

Early fruits can appear between ages 4‚Äì7 depending on region, genetics, and soil conditions. Mature female trees may produce hundreds of fruits in a season, though real-world yields outside the native range are still being studied.

MayaNut as a Superfood Ingredient

When roasted and ground, MayaNut forms a naturally sweet, slightly nutty powder that can be blended into drinks or used as a flour extender. It is caffeine-free, rich in complex carbohydrates, and pairs well with cocoa, cinnamon, vanilla, and plant-based milks.

Future products may include:

  • MayaNut hot or iced beverage mixes
  • Chocolate-MayaNut blends
  • MayaNut porridges or energy bowls
  • High-fiber bakery mixes and flatbreads

Agroforestry Potential

MayaNut fits naturally into multi-strata agroforestry systems. As a medium-to-large canopy tree, it can provide shade for cacao or coffee while forming the backbone of long-term perennial production.

Intercropping with fruit trees, nitrogen fixers, and perennial ground covers helps stabilize soil and improve organic matter. In regions where annual crops are losing viability due to climate stress, MayaNut may offer a durable alternative that still produces food year after year.

Recipes You Can Make With MayaNut

MayaNut powder is versatile and works in drinks, baked goods, flatbreads, and even desserts. Below are early recipe ideas designed around future MayaNut Company products.

MayaNut ‚ÄúCocoa-Style‚Äù Drink

Mix 1‚Äì2 tbsp MayaNut powder with hot water or warm plant milk. Add cocoa powder for a mocha-style drink. Sweeten with honey, sugar, or drink unsweetened for a more traditional flavor.

MayaNut Tortillas / Flatbreads

Combine 2 parts corn masa with 1 part MayaNut powder. Add water until it forms a workable dough. Press and cook on a hot skillet until lightly browned. Works well for sweet or savory fillings.

MayaNut Cookies

Replace 20‚Äì30% of all-purpose flour in a cookie recipe with MayaNut powder. The powder adds a warm, roasted note similar to chocolate or coffee.

Fresh MayaNut Fruit Jam

When fruits are available, remove seeds and cook the pulp with a splash of water, cinnamon, and sugar to taste. Simmer until thickened, mash gently, and store in sterilized jars. Pulp can also be frozen for later use.
