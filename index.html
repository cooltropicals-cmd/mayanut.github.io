ABOUT – TREE, GENETICS, AND LONG-TERM VISION

About The MayaNut Company

The MayaNut Company builds around a simple, stubborn idea: treat MayaNut like a long-term crop and a long-term relationship, not a quick trend. That means planting trees now, protecting their genetics, and slowly building products that keep those trees – and their story – relevant to everyday life.

Why this tree? Brosimum alicastrum is a canopy tree in the Moraceae family, related to figs, mulberries, and breadfruit. It forms dense crowns, deep roots, and fruits with a sweet outer pulp surrounding a starchy, roastable seed: MayaNut. In a warming, storm-prone world, a tall tree with strong roots and edible seeds is worth paying attention to.
South Florida as a living lab. Early plantings in South Florida treat every seedling as both a future shade tree and a data point. Which lines handle wind? Which shrug off salt haze or brief cool spells? The goal is to mix regional seed sources so plantations act like living archives rather than monocultures.
Genetic diversity as a directive. The project intentionally seeks a wide spread of seed sources, including both recognized subspecies (subsp. alicastrum and subsp. bolivarense) whenever possible. The long-term directive is simple: preserve a broad, well-documented genetic base while proving that MayaNut can carry its own weight as a modern superfood.
From “famine food” to deliberate choice. In some histories the tree is described as an emergency or famine food, called upon when other crops fail. Here the goal is different: make foods and drinks that people choose on purpose – not out of desperation – and in doing so keep the tree worth planting, protecting, and breeding for generations.

Brosimum alicastrum – Research & Superfood Background

The leaves of Brosimum alicastrum tell part of the story: a dense, evergreen canopy built for shade, rain, and long-term resilience in tropical and subtropical landscapes.

Across its native range, people know this species under many names. On this site we use the unified, modern term MayaNut for the edible seed, while still honoring the local words that kept the tree alive in memory.

Common Names & Indigenous Use

Brosimum alicastrum has many names. In English and Spanish it is often called MayaNut, nuez de ramón, or ojoche. In different regions it may be known as breadnut, ramón, ox / oox, guayumaro, and guaimaro.

Fruits were harvested and the seeds roasted, ground, or stored as reserve food. Roasted seeds can make a warm drink similar to a caffeine-free coffee or cocoa replacement, or they can be turned into flour for tortillas, breads, and porridges.

Natural Range & Climate

In the wild, Brosimum alicastrum grows from southern Mexico through Central America into parts of northern South America. It appears in lowland and foothill forests, tolerating both humid rainforest and seasonal dry forests.

Once established, the tree can handle long dry spells thanks to deep roots, yet also thrives in wetter climates – a combination that makes it attractive for tropical and subtropical agroforestry.

Growth, Roots & Hurricane Resistance

MayaNut trees can reach 20–40 meters in height under good conditions. Young trees often invest heavily in roots and trunk before showing dramatic canopy expansion.

Deep taproots and wide lateral roots make the species a strong candidate for hurricane-resilient plantings, especially in limestone-influenced or rocky soils where anchoring matters.

Agroforestry & Superfood Systems

In traditional farming landscapes, Brosimum alicastrum often appears as a scattered shade tree above crops or grazing. It can share space with cacao, coffee, fruit trees, or pasture.

For modern agroforestry, The MayaNut Company treats it as a superfood anchor tree: the stable canopy layer in systems where understory crops change over decades, but the MayaNut trees remain.

Ecology & Forest Role

MayaNut trees support insects, birds, and mammals, while deep shade and leaf litter help restore microclimates and soil moisture.

Because people value the seeds, there is a built-in incentive to keep canopies standing instead of clearing them – an alignment between food, income, and forest structure.

Domestication & Genetic Diversity

Globally, Brosimum alicastrum is still in relatively early stages of domestication. Local communities may recognize individual “good trees” but there is no large catalog of named cultivars yet.

This project actively seeks seed from many regions and both main subspecies, building a resilient, well-documented genetic base for future plantations and research.

MAYANUT FAQ & MINI ASSISTANT

MayaNut FAQ

Quick answers about MayaNut, Brosimum alicastrum, and how this project is being built for the long term – as both a superfood brand and a genetic safety net for the species.

What is MayaNut?

“MayaNut” is our modern name for the edible seed of Brosimum alicastrum, a tropical canopy tree in the Moraceae family. The roasted seed can be ground into a powder for drinks, tortillas, flatbreads, and baked goods. It is naturally caffeine-free.

Is this the same as ramón / ojoche / guaimaro?

Yes – in many cases those names point to the same tree. Across its native range people say ramón, ojoche, guayumaro, guaimaro, ox / oox, and more. We use “MayaNut” as a simple global name while respecting and referencing local terms.

When will products be for sale?

The first commercial MayaNut powder and ready-to-drink concepts are planned around the late 2020s. Trees must be planted, grown, and harvested carefully, which takes real time.

Why talk so much about genetics?

Because this is more than a drink idea. The aim is to keep Brosimum alicastrum visible as a species: which lines exist where, how they behave, and how they can be replanted as climates shift. A functioning superfood brand is one way to fund that work.

Can I grow MayaNut myself?

In frost-free tropical and subtropical climates it may be possible to grow Brosimum alicastrum. Always check local regulations before importing seeds or plants. The MayaNut Company hopes to share clearer guidance in the future.
