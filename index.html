Common Names & Synonyms

Brosimum alicastrum is known by many common names across its range. In English and Spanish it is often called maya nut, mayanut or nuez de ram√≥n. Other names include breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, guaimaro, and regional spellings such as guayumaro, oox or ox.

In parts of Colombia and Venezuela, the tree is also grouped with other ‚Äúpan de √°rbol‚Äù or bread-tree types, and the name gu√°imaro or ram√≥n may be used in everyday speech. Botanists recognize at least two subspecies: Brosimum alicastrum subsp. alicastrum, which is widespread through Mesoamerica, and Brosimum alicastrum subsp. bolivarense, described from northern South America.

All of these names generally refer to the same tree species and its edible seeds. Using ‚Äúmaya nut‚Äù and ‚ÄúBrosimum alicastrum‚Äù together helps people discover this underused tropical resource in search engines, scientific literature and traditional knowledge.

Natural Range & Climate

Brosimum alicastrum, commonly called maya nut, mayan breadnut, breadnut, ram√≥n, ojoche, or guaimaro, is native to the tropical and subtropical forests of Mesoamerica and northern South America. Its natural range extends from southern Mexico and Guatemala through much of Central America and into parts of Colombia and Venezuela.

In its home range the tree can be found from lowland rainforests near sea level up into mid-elevation cloud forests. It prefers warm, humid climates with distinct wet and dry seasons, and it tolerates a wide variety of soils as long as they are not permanently waterlogged.

For Florida, The MayaNut Company is especially interested in how far north the species can be grown reliably. Early evidence suggests that areas at or below about 27¬∞N (just south of Stuart, Florida) should be suitable for long-term cultivation, with the best success expected in the warmest parts of South Florida.

Growth

Maya nut is a medium to large evergreen tree, often reaching 20‚Äì35 m (65‚Äì100 ft) tall in favorable conditions. Young trees typically grow with a straight trunk and a dense, rounded canopy that becomes more spreading with age.

Seedlings establish best in filtered light or partial shade, then gradually adapt to more open conditions as they mature. Once rooted, the tree develops a strong, extensive root system that helps it tolerate seasonal droughts and anchor itself firmly in the soil.

Flowering and fruiting depend on local climate, but in many areas trees can bear annually once they reach maturity. The fruits ripen into small, yellow-orange drupes containing a single, nutritious seed ‚Äî the ‚Äúmaya nut‚Äù ‚Äî which can be dried, roasted, or ground into flour or beverage bases.

In Agriculture

Maya nut has strong potential as a multipurpose agroforestry tree. Its dense canopy provides deep, cool shade that can protect understory crops, livestock, or people from intense tropical sun. In traditional systems the tree has been grown with maize, cacao, and other crops, helping to stabilize soils and recycle nutrients through leaf litter.

The seeds themselves are a highly versatile product. They can be roasted to make a caffeine-free beverage, ground into flour for baking, or combined with other ingredients for new food and drink products. Leaves and fallen fruit have also been used as animal fodder in some regions.

For Florida, The MayaNut Company is exploring how maya nut can fit into modern tropical agriculture ‚Äî including mixed plantings, windbreaks, shade systems, and future commercial orchards focused on beverage, powder, and flour production.

Ecology & Forest Role

In its native forests, Brosimum alicastrum is an important food source for wildlife. Bats, birds, and terrestrial mammals feed on the fruit and help disperse the seeds. The tree‚Äôs deep roots and dense canopy contribute to soil stability, water regulation, and local microclimates.

Because it can fruit heavily and consistently, maya nut has been considered in some areas as a key species for forest restoration and community food security projects, helping bridge gaps between conservation and sustainable livelihoods.

Domestication

Brosimum alicastrum has not seen any large-scale commercial use as of 2025 in the United States, partly because the tree is truly tropical and only grows reliably at the southern tip of Florida, which limits its range significantly. Yet people in Central America rely on this wonder tree to survive during hard times. Mature trees, while surprisingly resilient, appear to be cold hardy to around 31¬∞F only for short periods and will often defoliate completely after strong cold events.

Perhaps if evolutionary pressure selected individuals with cold-hardy genes, the range of maya nut could be extended northward. While Morus rubra, a similar moraceous species, grows all the way up into Canada, Brosimum alicastrum can suffer from leaf wilt at temperatures as high as 50¬∞F if conditions are stressful, which is one reason it has not yet been commercialized in colder regions. The MayaNut Company is committed to ensuring that this species, Brosimum alicastrum, survives with enough genetic diversity to sustain new cultivars and future breeding programs.

As such, domesticating individuals with more ideal traits is necessary to improve this crop further. Indirect breeding may have already occurred over centuries of Maya and other Indigenous peoples selecting and planting the biggest seeds from favored trees, gradually favoring large-seed individuals. A focused breeding program for B. alicastrum could, in just a few generations, make real progress because trees can begin fruiting under ideal conditions in about 3‚Äì4 years. By selecting large-seed, low-fruit-ratio individuals and also selecting cold-hardy trees, it should be possible to create improved genetic cultivars for food, beverage and agroforestry use.

FAQ

What is maya nut?

Maya nut, sometimes written mayanut or maya-nut, is the seed of the tropical rainforest tree Brosimum alicastrum. The seed can be roasted and ground into a powder, then brewed into a rich, naturally caffeine-free beverage. If harvested fresh and separated from its fruity pulp, the seeds can go through nixtamalization (the same process corn must go through to become corn flour). Seeds may then be ground into a paste and made into tortillas or any baked goods like cookies and bread.

Is maya nut the same as breadnut, ojoche or ram√≥n?

Yes. In different regions, the tree and its seeds are known as maya nut, mayanut, breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n or guaimaro. They all refer to the same species, Brosimum alicastrum, or to its roasted seeds used for food and drink. In English, ‚Äúmayanut‚Äù as one word ‚Äî like jackfruit ‚Äî is a useful way to refer to this new plant and help people search for it online.

Can Brosimum alicastrum grow in Florida?

Early observations suggest that warm parts of Florida, especially South Florida at or below about 27¬∞N, can support this species. Our work focuses on proving how well maya nut can grow and produce in subtropical Florida conditions. No one has yet commercialized this species in the United States, and we, The MayaNut Company, chose Brosimum alicastrum as our candidate species for the revitalization of Florida agriculture and new agroforestry systems.
