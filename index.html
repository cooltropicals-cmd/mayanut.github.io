Common Names & Synonyms

Brosimum alicastrum is known by many common names across its range. In English and Spanish it is often called maya nut, mayanut, or nuez de ram√≥n. Other names include breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, guaimaro, and regional spellings such as guayumaro, oox or ox.

Natural Range & Climate

Brosimum alicastrum, commonly called maya nut, mayanut, breadnut, ram√≥n, ojoche, or guaimaro, is native to the tropical and subtropical forests of Mesoamerica and northern South America. Its natural range extends from southern Mexico and Guatemala through much of Central America and into parts of Colombia and Venezuela.

In its home range the tree can be found from lowland rainforests near sea level up into mid-elevation cloud forests. It prefers warm, humid climates with distinct wet and dry seasons, and it tolerates a wide variety of soils as long as they are not permanently waterlogged.

For Florida, The MayaNut Company is especially interested in how far north the species can be grown reliably. Early evidence suggests that areas at or below about 27¬∞N (just south of Stuart, Florida) should be suitable for long-term cultivation, with the best success expected in the warmest parts of South Florida.

Growth

Maya nut is a medium to large evergreen tree, often reaching 20‚Äì35 m (65‚Äì100 ft) tall in favorable conditions. Young trees typically grow with a straight trunk and a dense, rounded canopy that becomes more spreading with age.

Seedlings establish best in filtered light or partial shade, then gradually adapt to more open conditions as they mature. Once rooted, the tree develops a strong, extensive root system that helps it tolerate seasonal droughts and anchor itself firmly in the soil.

Flowering and fruiting depend on local climate, but in many areas trees can bear annually once they reach maturity. The fruits ripen into small, yellow-orange drupes containing a single, nutritious seed ‚Äî the ‚Äúmaya nut‚Äù or ‚Äúbreadnut‚Äù ‚Äî which can be dried, roasted, or ground into flour or beverage bases.

In Agriculture

Maya nut has strong potential as a multipurpose agroforestry tree. Its dense canopy provides deep, cool shade that can protect understory crops, livestock, or people from intense tropical sun. In traditional systems the tree has been grown with maize, cacao, and other crops, helping to stabilize soils and recycle nutrients through leaf litter.

The seeds themselves are a highly versatile product. They can be roasted to make a caffeine-free beverage, ground into flour for baking, or combined with other ingredients for new food and drink products. Leaves and fallen fruit have also been used as animal fodder in some regions.

For Florida, The MayaNut Company is exploring how maya nut can fit into modern tropical agriculture ‚Äî including mixed plantings, windbreaks, shade systems, and future commercial orchards focused on beverage, powder, and flour production from Brosimum alicastrum.

Domestication & Breeding

Brosimum alicastrum has not yet seen any true large-scale commercial use in the United States as of 2025. One reason is that the tree is strongly tropical, growing naturally only in the warmest parts of Florida, which limits its range and makes domestication more challenging. People across Central America have relied on maya nut and breadnut to survive for centuries, but the species has not been fully developed as a modern crop in North America.

Mature trees are thought to be cold hardy only down to about 31 ¬∞F (‚Äì0.5 ¬∞C) for short periods, and they may completely defoliate after a strong cold event. In contrast, Morus rubra, another member of the Moraceae family, can grow far into temperate regions and up toward Canada. Brosimum alicastrum can suffer from leaf wilt at temperatures as high as 50 ¬∞F (10 ¬∞C) if the trees are stressed, which helps explain why it has not yet been widely commercialized.

If evolutionary pressure and careful selection focused on individuals with more cold-hardy genes, its range could potentially be extended northward. The MayaNut Company wants to ensure that this species, Brosimum alicastrum, survives with enough genetic diversity to support new cultivars in the future. Domestication will depend on selecting individuals with ideal traits: larger seeds, desirable flavor, reliable yields, and better tolerance of cool snaps.

Indirect breeding may have already occurred over centuries as Mayan communities planted or favored trees with bigger seeds, effectively selecting large-seed individuals. A dedicated breeding program for B. alicastrum could accelerate this process. Under ideal tropical conditions, trees may begin fruiting in as little as 3‚Äì4 years, which means that in just a few generations breeders could select large-seed, low-pulp individuals and introduce more cold-hardy genes into named cultivars. The long-term vision of The MayaNut Company is to help guide this domestication process while preserving broad genetic diversity.

Ecology & Forest Role

In its native forests, Brosimum alicastrum is an important food source for wildlife. Bats, birds, and terrestrial mammals feed on the fruit and help disperse the seeds. The tree‚Äôs deep roots and dense canopy contribute to soil stability, water regulation, and local microclimates.

Because it can fruit heavily and consistently, maya nut has been considered in some areas as a key species for forest restoration and community food security projects, helping bridge gaps between conservation and sustainable livelihoods. Its role as a long-lived canopy tree makes it valuable for climate resilience and landscape-level restoration efforts.

FAQ

What is maya nut?

Maya nut is the seed of the mysterious, tropical rainforest tree Brosimum alicastrum. The seed can be roasted and ground into a powder, then brewed into a rich, naturally caffeine-free beverage. If harvested fresh and separated from its fruity pulp, the seeds can go through nixtamalization, the very same process corn must go through to become corn flour. Seeds may then be ground into a paste and made into tortillas or any baked goods like cookies and bread. In many regions this same plant is also called mayanut, breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, or guaimaro.

Is maya nut the same as breadnut, ojoche or ram√≥n?

Yes. In different regions, the tree and its seeds are known as maya nut, mayanut, breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, or guaimaro. They all refer to the same species, Brosimum alicastrum, or to its roasted seeds used for food and drink. For English, ‚Äúmayanut‚Äù as one word ‚Äî just like jackfruit ‚Äî can be used to refer to this new plant and its products.

Can Brosimum alicastrum grow in Florida?

No one has yet come along to commercialize this species in America, and we, The MayaNut Company, choose Brosimum alicastrum as our candidate species for the revitalization of Florida agriculture and future tropical agroforestry systems.
