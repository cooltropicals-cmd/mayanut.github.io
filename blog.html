Research

Brosimum alicastrum is a species of tree in the family Moraceae. This species is primarily dominant in the tropical forests of southern Mexico, but it does occur in more sporadic populations all the way north to Tamaulipas, and in western parts of Mexico and Central America, extending into northern South America, as well as parts of the Caribbean, including Cuba and Jamaica, possibly through deliberate human seed migration for agricultural or religious reasons.

Historically, the species Brosimum alicastrum is believed to have been an important part of not just the Maya people but probably many other cultures overlapping its range. The tree was probably used by early Mesoamerican hunter-gatherers as early as 10,000 B.C. More recently in history, however, there is very little, if any, direct evidence of its specific uses in ancient cultures. No known texts or art clearly describe recipes or uses in their culture.

Today, it is used as a fodder tree for ranchers in Mexico and to make tortillas, bread, and even a coffee-like beverage, all made from the seeds of this ancient rainforest tree. The MayaNut Company is working in South Florida to establish America's first Brosimum alicastrum farm, as this species is widely underutilized.
