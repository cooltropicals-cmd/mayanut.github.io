About The MayaNut Company

The MayaNut Company was founded in 2025 with a simple but ambitious goal: bring Brosimum alicastrum back into the spotlight and give this ancient tree a home in modern agriculture. For founder August Kokus, the project is deeply personal. He has always believed that the most meaningful legacy a person can leave is something that keeps adapting long after we’re gone. A piece of life that grows, evolves, and continues telling your story. For him, that legacy is a tree — one that has fed civilizations, survived climate swings, and stood longer than empires. The idea that a single species could continue thriving for centuries, remembering him in its own quiet way, is what inspired The MayaNut Company.

Brosimum alicastrum, known as Maya nut, has a long and remarkable history. Its native range stretches across Cuba, Jamaica, the Yucatán Peninsula, Belize, Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, Panama, and into the northern parts of Colombia and Venezuela. Humans likely first encountered the tree roughly 15,000–20,000 years ago, as the earliest Paleo-Indian groups moved south into the tropical Americas — give or take a few thousand years, depending on the route and timing. Thousands of years later, the Maya and other Indigenous peoples relied on the tree for food, shade, and cultural significance. Its seeds were ground into flour, roasted for drinks, eaten plain, and stored as an emergency food during droughts and seasonal shortages. Many ancient Maya sites still have Maya nut trees growing around them today, a living echo of that relationship.

Today, the seed is still incredibly versatile. When roasted, it develops a warm, nutty flavor that works beautifully in baked goods or as a caffeine-free coffee alternative. Ground Maya nut can be blended into drinks, smoothies, and porridges, or used as a nutrient-rich flour. Our work at The MayaNut Company focuses on establishing South Florida’s first Maya nut plantation, bringing this underappreciated species into a new agricultural region where it can thrive. We are selecting strong genetics, learning how the trees respond to Florida’s subtropical climate, and introducing people to this forgotten superfood one seedling at a time.

Looking ahead, we’re developing the first line of MayaNut beverages in the United States. Our plan is to sell roasted, ground Maya nut for brewing at home — smooth, rich, and naturally caffeine-free — along with pre-bottled MayaNut drinks. If all goes well, our bottled beverages will begin appearing in grocery stores around 2028, marking a new chapter for an ancient tree. This is more than a product launch; it’s the beginning of a new agricultural story for South Florida and a chance to revive one of the most resilient food plants in the Americas.
