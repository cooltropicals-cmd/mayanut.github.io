Brosimum alicastrum

Maya Nut Research & Field Notes

Exploring the range, growth, morphology, and agricultural potential of Brosimum alicastrum ‚Äî also known as Maya nut, breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, or guiamaro.

RESEARCH

Range

Brosimum alicastrum is a species of tree in the family Moraceae. This species is primarily dominant in the tropical forests of southern Mexico, but it also occurs in more sporadic populations as far north as Tamaulipas, and in western parts of Mexico and Central America, extending into northern South America, as well as parts of the Caribbean, including Cuba and Jamaica. In different regions, it is also known as Maya nut, breadnut, ojoche, ram√≥n, or guiamaro.

Growth

The tree can grow up to around 40 meters tall in its native range, but more typically reaches 25‚Äì35 meters. In South Florida, several observed trees do not usually exceed about 20 meters, likely in response to roughly 65 inches of average annual rainfall and local growing conditions. Seedlings do not respond well to full sun ‚Äî their leaves begin to turn yellow, signaling that the UV intensity is too high. Seedlings prefer to be kept moist and partially shaded, only tolerating stronger light once they are taller than about 2‚Äì3 feet. At that point, they can also tolerate periods of drought more reliably.

Tree Lifecycle & Yield

Adult trees in full sun may still perform well, but their leaves often take on a more yellow-green tint instead of a deep, healthy green. Trees can begin flowering as early as four years old, but rarely enter into consistently heavy seed production until roughly a decade of age. These traits indicate that the species is best treated as a long-term perennial investment rather than a short-rotation crop, with yield increasing as the individual tree matures.

In Agriculture

One of the main reasons Brosimum alicastrum has not been cultivated widely in the United States is the combination of limited infrastructure, limited technical knowledge, and the long time required to reach high yields. Unlike many annual crops, Brosimum alicastrum does not depreciate with age. In fact, the larger the tree becomes, the more it tends to produce. It does not need to be replanted and re-fertilized in the same way as many annual row crops. Instead, its extensive root system can tap into the water table below ground and anchor the tree against wind and storms.

Growing naturally in places such as the Yucat√°n and other tropical regions, this species has evolved alongside intense seasonal storms, including hurricanes. That history is one reason it is so promising for Florida, where wind resistance and deep rooting are valuable traits in agricultural systems.

Morphology

Brosimum alicastrum belongs to the Moraceae family, which means its flowers are numerous, small, and aggregated into a globose, fused inflorescence. Plants are usually dioecious (separate male and female individuals), but monoecious trees also occur. The flower heads of Brosimum resemble miniature versions of Treculia inflorescences. Phylogenetic and biogeographic work suggests that the lineage originated from an African ancestor, with seeds or plant material successfully rafting and colonizing the Americas. Most other members of the tribe Dorstenieae are still found in Africa, Asia, and Australia.

In Brosimum, a single seed is typically present in each fruit, whereas the ancestral form may have produced several seeds in a small, ball-shaped mass surrounded by peltate bracts. Over evolutionary time, Brosimum appears to have shifted toward producing one central pistillate flower that develops into the seed, with surrounding male flowers in monoecious individuals, or a single pistillate flower without surrounding male flowers in strictly female trees. Environmental stress can also influence which sex a given individual expresses over its lifetime.

Genetic Diversity & Domestication

More research is needed on this species in local populations across its range. The MayaNut Company is actively working on acquiring new Brosimum alicastrum seeds from different genetic sources to ensure there is enough genetic diversity for this species to perform well in future agricultural systems.

Domestication by the Mayan culture appears to have been limited, and the species Brosimum alicastrum could be selectively bred into more stable, named strains that yield larger or more nutritious seeds, or show improved performance in specific climates.

Historical Context

Historically, Brosimum alicastrum is believed to have been important not only to the Maya people, but likely to many other cultures overlapping its range. The tree may have been used by early Mesoamerican hunter-gatherers as far back as 10,000 B.C. In more recent history, however, there is very little direct evidence documenting specific recipes or uses in ancient cultures. No known texts or artwork clearly spell out how the seeds or fruit were prepared.

Culinary Uses

Today, Maya nut is used as a fodder tree for ranchers in parts of Mexico, and the seeds can be used to make cookies, tortillas, bread, and even a coffee-like beverage ‚Äî all produced from this ancient tropical rainforest tree. The MayaNut Company is working in South Florida to establish a dedicated Brosimum alicastrum farm, often referred to as a Maya nut plantation, as this species remains widely underutilized in modern agroforestry systems.

Another edible part of the plant is the fruit. It starts off green, then fades to orange, and ripens to a deep red. At the green stage, the pulp is hard ‚Äî almost woody ‚Äî with a flavor reminiscent of bitter latex. As the fruit turns orange, it becomes softer and noticeably sweeter, usually still attached firmly to the tree. Only when the fruit reaches a dark, crimson red does it soften enough to slip cleanly off the seed.

At full ripeness, the texture is similar to chewy candy or tamarind, with a deep, syrup-like sweetness similar to very ripe figs or raisins, followed by a gentle tang. The fruit has almost no shelf-life and must be eaten directly from the tree. Realistically, the only way consumers far from the tree could experience it would be through jams, jellies, or preserves made from freshly harvested fruit.

The seed, by contrast, can be stored for several months under proper conditions, and is the part most likely to form the basis of long-term food products such as roasted beverages, flours, and baking blends.
